# East Asia -- Death, Default, and Floor

## Comparative Overview

Japan, China, and South Korea share a common thread: all three legal systems were shaped by the Germanic civil law tradition, though each filtered that influence through distinct cultural and political histories. Japan directly adopted the German BGB model during the Meiji Restoration (1868-1912). Korea inherited its civil code structure from Japan during the colonial period (1910-1945) and substantially retained it post-independence. China drew from both German and Soviet models, though the Soviet influence dominated during the Mao era before the 2021 Civil Code reestablished a more clearly Germanic framework.

The forced share provisions across all three systems -- iryubun (Japan), yuryubun (Korea), and the mandatory provision (China) -- trace directly to the German Pflichtteil. The structural logic is identical: the testator cannot fully disinherit the innermost family circle.

The critical finding for the death-as-trigger-event framework is Japan. Until April 1, 2026, Japan was the only developed democracy that did not permit joint custody after divorce. One parent received sole parental authority (shinken). The other parent was functionally erased from the legal family structure. This is structurally identical to the "parental death" described in the framework -- a living person is treated as legally dead with respect to their children.

# JAPAN

## System Overview

Japan operates under the Minpo (Civil Code), first enacted in 1896-1898, modeled primarily on the first draft of the German BGB. The Civil Code was substantially revised during the American occupation in 1947-1948 to eliminate the feudal ie (household) system and primogeniture, but the Germanic structural logic -- codified defaults, forced heirship, hierarchical succession orders -- was preserved. Book IV governs Family (Shinzoku), Book V governs Succession (Sozoku).

Key structural features:

- **Codified defaults.** Statutory shares are precisely defined for every family configuration. No judicial discretion in the base allocation.
- **Forced heirship (iryubun).** Children and spouses receive a mandatory share that cannot be eliminated by will.
- **Koseki system.** All family events are recorded in a centralized family register. Legal existence of family relationships depends on koseki registration.
- **Post-divorce sole custody (until April 2026).** Only one parent received parental authority after divorce. This was the most restrictive custody system in the developed world.
- **2024 reform.** Joint custody became available effective April 1, 2026. The century-long sole-custody rule has ended, but enforcement and cultural adoption remain open questions.

## Biological Death Defaults

### Inheritance (Civil Code, Book V -- Sozoku)

#### Who Inherits (Articles 886-895)

Japanese intestacy law organizes heirs into three classes. The surviving spouse always inherits alongside whichever class of blood relatives exists. Only one class inherits at a time -- the existence of any heir in a higher-priority class excludes all lower classes.

| Priority | Who | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Always | Surviving spouse (haigusha) -- co-inherits with whichever class is present | Art. 890 |
| First | Children (ko) of the deceased. Grandchildren inherit by representation if a child has predeceased. | Art. 887 |
| Second | Parents (chokuei sonzoku -- lineal ascendants). Grandparents if parents are deceased. | Art. 889(1)(i) |
| Third | Siblings (kyodai shimai) of the deceased. Nieces/nephews inherit by representation. | Art. 889(1)(ii) |

**Special provisions:**

- A fetus is treated as already born for inheritance purposes, provided the child is subsequently born alive (Art. 886).
- Adopted children (yoshiko) have the same inheritance rights as biological children.
- Since a 2013 Supreme Court ruling, children born out of wedlock receive equal inheritance shares to those born in wedlock (previously they received only half).

#### Statutory Shares (Article 900)

The spouse's share increases as the blood-relative class descends. This is the mirror image of the German system, where the spouse's share is smallest alongside children and largest alongside remote relatives.

| Family Configuration | Spouse's Share | Blood Relatives' Share | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spouse + children | **1/2** | Children split remaining **1/2** equally | Art. 900(i) |
| Spouse + parents | **2/3** | Parents split remaining **1/3** equally | Art. 900(ii) |
| Spouse + siblings | **3/4** | Siblings split remaining **1/4** equally | Art. 900(iii) |
| Spouse alone (no blood relatives) | **Entire estate** | -- | Art. 890 |
| No spouse, children exist | -- | Children inherit entire estate equally | Art. 887, 900 |

**Example:** Decedent leaves an estate of JPY 100 million, a surviving spouse, and three children. The spouse receives 1/2 (JPY 50 million). The three children split the remaining 1/2 equally (JPY 16.67 million each).

**Comparison to Germany:** Under BGB default (Zugewinngemeinschaft), the spouse alongside children receives 1/2 (1/4 statutory + 1/4 gain equalization). Japan gives the same 1/2 to the spouse but without the matrimonial property regime calculation. The Japanese system is simpler -- no Zugewinngemeinschaft adjustment is needed because the share is fixed by statute.

### The Iryubun -- Compulsory Portion (Articles 1042-1049)

The iryubun (遺留分) is Japan's forced heirship mechanism, directly modeled on the German Pflichtteil. It guarantees certain heirs a minimum share of the estate that cannot be overridden by testamentary disposition.

#### Who Gets It

| Entitled Person | Iryubun Amount | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Children (direct descendants) | **1/2** of their intestate share | Art. 1042(1)(ii) |
| Surviving spouse | **1/2** of their intestate share | Art. 1042(1)(ii) |
| Parents / lineal ascendants (only if no children exist) | **1/3** of their intestate share | Art. 1042(1)(i) |

**Not entitled:** Siblings. This matches the German rule exactly -- only the innermost family circle is protected.

#### Practical Calculations

| Family Configuration | Spouse's Intestate Share | Spouse's Iryubun | Each Child's Intestate Share (2 children) | Each Child's Iryubun |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Spouse + 2 children | 1/2 | **1/4** | 1/4 | **1/8** |
| Spouse + 1 child | 1/2 | **1/4** | 1/2 | **1/4** |
| Spouse + parents (no children) | 2/3 | **1/3** | -- | -- |
| Parents only (no spouse, no children) | -- | -- | -- | Parents' iryubun = 1/3 of 1/2 each = **1/6** each |

#### Nature of the Right

- Like the German Pflichtteil, the iryubun was reformed in 2018 to become a **monetary claim** (iryubun sammatsu seikyuken) rather than a right to specific assets. The 2018 amendment clarified that the iryubun holder is a creditor, not a co-heir.
- The claim arises at the moment of death and must be exercised within one year of knowledge of the infringement, or ten years from the opening of succession.
- Gifts made by the decedent within the ten years before death are added back to the estate for iryubun calculation (Art. 1044).
- An heir may renounce their iryubun right, but renunciation before the opening of succession requires court approval (Art. 1049).

#### Grounds for Denial

Under Article 892, the testator may disinherit a presumptive heir (haijo) only on narrow grounds:

- Abuse or serious insult toward the decedent
- Serious criminal conduct
- Other grave misconduct

A family court must approve the disinheritance. As in Germany, mere estrangement or disagreement is insufficient.

---

### 2018 Amendment: Spouse's Right of Residence (Haigusha Kyojuken)

The 2018 inheritance reform (effective April 2020) introduced two new rights for surviving spouses:

| Right | Duration | Effect | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Short-term right of residence (tanki kyojuken) | Minimum 6 months after death | Spouse may continue living in the shared residence without charge, regardless of who inherits the property | Art. 1037 |
| Long-term right of residence (haigusha kyojuken) | Potentially lifetime | By testamentary disposition or partition agreement, the spouse may be granted a right to reside for life. The right is valued lower than ownership, so the spouse retains more liquid assets from the estate. | Art. 1028-1036 |

**Significance for the framework:** This amendment addresses a specific failure mode in the prior system. Before 2018, a surviving spouse might inherit the house but receive no cash (because the house consumed their entire statutory share). The new right separates occupancy from ownership, ensuring the surviving spouse is not displaced by the death event. This is an example of the system patching a discovered gap in its default matrix.

## Parental Death: Custody After Divorce

### The Old Rule: Sole Parental Authority (Pre-April 2026)

Article 819 of the Civil Code, as it stood from 1947 to March 31, 2026, mandated:

- **During marriage:** Joint parental authority (kyodo shinken). Both parents share authority over the child. (Art. 818)
- **After divorce:** ONLY ONE parent receives parental authority (Art. 819). The parents must agree on who. If they cannot agree, the family court decides. There was no option for joint custody.

This was the most restrictive custody system in any developed democracy. The non-custodial parent had no legally enforceable right to regular contact. In practice, the custodial parent (overwhelmingly the mother -- approximately 84% of cases) could and routinely did terminate all contact between the child and the other parent.

**Structural equivalence to death:** Under the old Article 819, divorce triggered an outcome functionally identical to one parent dying. The non-custodial parent was erased from the child's legal family structure. The koseki system recorded this change. The non-custodial parent had no parental authority, limited or no enforceable visitation, and weak child support enforcement. The child lost one parent by operation of law, not by biology.

### Koseki (Family Register) System

The koseki (戸籍) is Japan's civil registration system, maintained by municipal governments. Unlike Western individual-centric civil registration, the koseki is family-unit-centric. It records:

- Full names of all family members
- Dates of birth
- Parent-child relationships
- Marriages and divorces
- Adoptions
- Deaths
- Parental authority designations
- Disinheritance of presumed heirs

A marriage, divorce, birth, or death is not legally effective until recorded in the koseki. The register is the authoritative record of legal family status. Upon divorce, the transfer of sole parental authority was recorded in the koseki, making the non-custodial parent's legal erasure a matter of public record.

### International Criticism: Hague Convention

Japan ratified the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction on April 1, 2014, under sustained international pressure. However, Japan's compliance has been widely criticized:

- Japan's sole-custody system was fundamentally incompatible with the Hague Convention's premise that children should maintain relationships with both parents.
- In 2019, the leaders of France, Germany, and Italy jointly condemned Japan's position on international child abduction.
- The United Nations Human Rights Council launched a formal complaint accusing Japan of violating international law on children's rights.
- In practice, a Japanese parent (typically the mother) who took a child to Japan could rely on the sole-custody system to obtain exclusive parental authority, effectively ratifying the abduction through domestic law.

Japan has been described as a "black hole" for international child abduction -- children taken to Japan were rarely returned, because domestic law provided no mechanism for shared custody that the Hague Convention presumes.

### The 2024 Reform: Joint Custody Becomes Available

On May 17, 2024, the Diet passed Act No. 33 of 2024, amending the Civil Code to permit joint parental authority after divorce. The amendment was promulgated on May 24, 2024 and **came into force on April 1, 2026**.

Key provisions of the amended Article 819:

| Feature | Old Rule (pre-April 2026) | New Rule (from April 2026) |
|---|---|---|
| Post-divorce parental authority | Sole custody mandatory. Only one parent. | Joint custody available. Parents may agree on joint or sole. |
| Mutual agreement divorce | Parents must designate one parent | Parents may designate one or both parents |
| Court-decided divorce | Court designates one parent | Court may designate one or both parents, based on best interests |
| Mandatory sole custody exception | N/A | Court MUST assign sole custody if domestic violence, child abuse, or risk of harm to child exists |
| Existing divorces | N/A | Either parent may petition to modify from sole to joint custody |

**Critical assessment:** The reform is significant but comes with caveats:

1. **Cultural inertia.** Over a century of sole-custody practice has created deep cultural expectations. Voluntary joint custody may remain rare in the near term.
2. **Enforcement gap.** Japan has historically lacked meaningful enforcement of visitation orders. The reform does not create a robust enforcement mechanism comparable to contempt powers in US or European courts.
3. **DV exception is double-edged.** The mandatory sole-custody exception for domestic violence could be invoked strategically, as similar provisions have been elsewhere.

### Child Support

Child support in Japan is calculated using a standardized formula based on both parents' incomes and the child's age. The family court publishes reference tables (santeihyo) for calculation.

**Enforcement is notoriously weak:**

- A 2021 Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare survey found that only approximately 28% of single-mother households actually received child support payments.
- Only about 47% had formal child support agreements.
- Japan has no equivalent of the US income-withholding order system or the IV-D enforcement apparatus.
- The 2024 reform introduced a provision allowing priority seizure of a non-paying parent's assets with a single seizure order covering all future installments (effective April 2026), but this remains far weaker than automatic wage garnishment.

**Comparison to the US Title IV-D system:** The gap is enormous. The US has a federal-state enforcement infrastructure with income withholding, tax refund intercept, license suspension, passport denial, and contempt of court. Japan has none of these. A Japanese child support order is closer to a suggestion than a mandate.

## Financial Death

### Personal Insolvency

Japan has two primary personal insolvency mechanisms:

| Procedure | Japanese Name | Purpose | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bankruptcy (liquidation) | Hasan (破産) | Complete liquidation and discharge of debts | Bankruptcy Act (Hasan-ho), 2004 |
| Civil rehabilitation | Minji saisei (民事再生) | Reorganization with partial debt reduction; debtor retains assets | Civil Rehabilitation Act, 1999 |

**Treatment of child support:**

Child support and alimony obligations are **non-dischargeable** in Japanese bankruptcy. These claims survive bankruptcy and remain enforceable. Additionally, child support and alimony are given priority over general unsecured debts in insolvency distributions. A creditor holding a child support enforcement title can, with a single seizure order, attach not only past-due installments but also all future installments as they become due.

**Significance for the framework:** Japan correctly classifies child support as a non-dischargeable obligation -- the "financial death" of bankruptcy does not extinguish the parental obligation. However, since enforcement is weak even outside bankruptcy, the non-dischargeability is somewhat theoretical. The floor exists in law but lacks the enforcement infrastructure to make it operative.

# CHINA

## System Overview

China operates under the Civil Code of the People's Republic of China, which came into force on January 1, 2021. The Civil Code consolidated and replaced seven previously separate laws, including the 1985 Succession Law and the 2001 Marriage Law. Book V governs Family (Marriage and Family), Book VI governs Succession.

China's legal system was influenced by both the German civil law tradition (through Japan, during the Republican era) and Soviet legal theory (during the Mao era). The 2021 Civil Code represents a return toward the Germanic codification model, with clearly structured books, articles, and hierarchical default rules.

Key structural features:

- **Codified defaults.** Succession order and shares are defined by statute, with limited judicial discretion.
- **Mandatory provision for vulnerable heirs.** A testator must reserve a necessary portion for heirs who lack the ability to work and have no income source -- a targeted forced heirship provision narrower than the German Pflichtteil.
- **No personal bankruptcy (nationally).** Until pilot programs began in 2020-2021, natural persons in China had no formal insolvency mechanism. This creates a "no exit" scenario that is unique among major economies.
- **State influence.** Family law in China reflects state policy objectives (population control, social stability) to a degree not seen in other civil law systems.

---

## Biological Death Defaults

### Inheritance (Civil Code 2021, Book VI -- Succession)

#### Order of Succession (Article 1127)

| Priority | Who | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| First order | Spouse, children, and parents -- all inherit simultaneously | Unlike Japan/Germany, no sub-priority within the first order. All three categories share equally. |
| Second order | Siblings, paternal grandparents, and maternal grandparents | Inherit only if no first-order heirs exist |

**Definitions (Art. 1127):**
- "Children" includes children born in or out of wedlock, adopted children, and stepchildren raised by the decedent.
- "Parents" includes biological parents, adoptive parents, and stepparents who raised the decedent.
- "Siblings" includes full siblings, half-siblings, adopted siblings, and stepsiblings who supported or were supported by the decedent.

**Key difference from Japan/Germany:** The Chinese system does not separate the spouse from blood relatives into a parallel track. Instead, the spouse is a first-order heir alongside children and parents. In Japan and Germany, the spouse always co-inherits alongside whichever blood-relative class exists, with the share varying by class. In China, the spouse simply shares the first order with children and parents.

#### Distribution of Shares (Article 1130)

The general rule is equal shares among same-order heirs. However, Article 1130 provides for adjustment:

| Principle | Effect |
|---|---|
| Equal distribution (general rule) | Same-order heirs receive equal shares |
| Greater contribution | An heir who made predominant contributions to supporting the decedent may receive a larger share |
| Dependent heirs | An heir who lived with the decedent may receive a larger share |
| Neglectful heirs | An heir who had the ability and position to support the decedent but failed to do so shall receive no share or a smaller share |

**Significance for the framework:** The Chinese system introduces a performance-based adjustment to the default. The German/Japanese systems fix shares mechanically. China's system allows the court to reward actual caregiving and penalize neglect. This creates a softer floor -- the default is equal shares, but the floor can be lowered to zero for a neglectful heir.

#### Mandatory Provision (Article 1141)

Article 1141 provides: "Reservation of a necessary portion of an estate shall be made in a will for a successor who has neither the ability to work nor the source of income."

This is China's forced heirship provision, but it is narrower than the German Pflichtteil or Japanese iryubun in critical respects:

| Feature | China (Art. 1141) | Germany (Pflichtteil) | Japan (Iryubun) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Who is protected | Only heirs who lack ability to work AND have no income source | All children, spouse, parents (if no children) | All children, spouse, parents (if no children) |
| Amount | "Necessary portion" -- no fixed fraction | 1/2 of intestate share | 1/2 of intestate share (1/3 for parents alone) |
| Condition | Must demonstrate need (disability, poverty, minority) | Unconditional -- protected regardless of wealth or need | Unconditional |

**Significance for the framework:** China's mandatory provision protects only the most vulnerable heirs -- those who cannot support themselves. A wealthy adult child can be entirely disinherited. This is a needs-based floor rather than a status-based floor. The German/Japanese systems provide a status-based floor: you get the Pflichtteil/iryubun because you are a child, regardless of whether you need it.

#### Freedom of Testation (Article 1133)

A citizen may make a will to dispose of his or her personal property, subject to the mandatory provision of Article 1141. This establishes broad testamentary freedom, limited only by the needs-based floor.

### Changes from the 1985 Succession Law

The 2021 Civil Code replaced the 1985 Succession Law (which had been largely unchanged for 36 years). Key changes:

| Feature | 1985 Law | 2021 Civil Code |
|---|---|---|
| Will forms | Handwritten, notarized, witnessed, oral (emergency only). Notarized will given automatic priority. | Added printed wills and video wills. Removed automatic priority of notarized wills -- latest will now prevails. |
| Estate administration | No formal administrator system. Property transferred directly to heirs. | Introduced estate administrator (yichan guanliren) system with defined powers, duties, and accountability (Arts. 1145-1149). |
| Grounds for disinheritance | Killing the decedent, other heirs; abandonment/torture of decedent; forging/tampering with will | Added: concealing a will causing serious consequences; fraud/coercion affecting will-making causing serious consequences |
| Digital assets | Not addressed | Implicitly covered under personal property provisions |
| Forgiveness | Not addressed | Added: if an heir repents and the decedent subsequently forgives them, the heir may regain inheritance rights |

## Parental Death: Custody After Divorce

### Custody Framework (Civil Code, Book V -- Family)

| Article | Provision |
|---|---|
| Art. 1084 | After divorce, the parent-child relationship does not terminate. Both parents retain rights and obligations toward the child. Children under age two generally live with the mother. For children over two, the parents may agree; if not, the court decides based on best interests. |
| Art. 1085 | The non-custodial parent shall pay child support in part or in whole. Amount and duration determined by agreement or court judgment. The child may make additional demands when necessary. |
| Art. 1086 | The non-custodial parent has visitation rights. The custodial parent must facilitate visits. Schedule determined by agreement or court order. Visitation may be suspended if it harms the child's physical or mental health, and resumed when the grounds disappear. |

**Comparison to Japan's old system:** China's system is structurally more protective of the non-custodial parent than Japan's pre-2026 rule. Article 1084 explicitly preserves the parent-child relationship after divorce and gives the non-custodial parent visitation rights by statute. Japan's old Article 819 gave the non-custodial parent no statutory right to the child at all.

**Comparison to Germany:** China lacks Germany's presumption of joint custody. The default in Chinese divorces is that one parent has physical custody, though both retain parental obligations. Germany starts from joint custody and requires a court finding to move to sole custody.

### Enforcement of Child Support

Child support enforcement in China operates through the People's Courts:

- The court may order wage deduction from the non-custodial parent's employer.
- Non-compliance with a court order can result in fines, detention (up to 15 days), or forced execution of assets.
- In practice, enforcement is uneven, particularly in rural areas and across provincial boundaries.
- There is no centralized child support enforcement agency comparable to the US IV-D system or the South Korean Child Support Agency.

### One-Child Policy Legacy

The one-child policy (1979-2015) created distinctive inheritance and custody dynamics:

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Concentrated inheritance** | Each only child inherits the combined wealth of two parents and potentially four grandparents. The "4-2-1" structure: one child supporting two parents and four grandparents. |
| **Heightened custody stakes** | With only one child, custody disputes carry existential weight for both parents and grandparents. There is no "splitting" custody across multiple children. |
| **Gender imbalance** | Son preference combined with one-child restriction led to sex-selective abortion, creating a male-surplus generation. This affects marriage markets, which in turn affects inheritance and custody patterns. |
| **Grandparent dependence** | The 4-2-1 structure makes grandparents materially dependent on the single grandchild, increasing their stake in custody and inheritance outcomes. |

The policy was replaced by a two-child policy (2016) and then a three-child policy (2021), but the cohorts born under the one-child policy (approximately 1979-2015) will continue to drive inheritance patterns for decades.

## Financial Death

### Enterprise Bankruptcy Law (2006)

The Enterprise Bankruptcy Law (Qiye Pochan Fa) applies only to enterprise legal persons (companies) and certain unincorporated organizations. It does **not** apply to natural persons. This is a critical structural gap.

### Personal Bankruptcy: The "No Exit" Problem

China has historically had **no national personal bankruptcy system**. This means:

| Situation | US/Germany/Japan | China |
|---|---|---|
| Individual overwhelmed by debt | Can file for bankruptcy; receive discharge; restart | No formal mechanism. Debts persist indefinitely. |
| Failed entrepreneur with personal guarantees | Bankruptcy discharge available | Debts follow the person permanently. Added to "deadbeat" blacklist (shixin beizhixingren). |
| Judgment debtor | Can seek insolvency protection | Court enforcement continues; assets seized but no discharge of remainder |

**The "deadbeat" blacklist system (laolai):** Chinese courts maintain a publicly searchable database of judgment debtors who have failed to comply with court orders. Inclusion on this list triggers:

- Restrictions on air travel and high-speed rail
- Restrictions on luxury consumption
- Potential restrictions on children's enrollment in private schools
- Social stigma

This system functions as an enforcement mechanism but provides **no exit**. A person on the blacklist cannot discharge their debts through bankruptcy. They remain in permanent legal limbo.

### Pilot Programs (2020-2021 onward)

| Jurisdiction | Start | Key Features | Status (2026) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wenzhou | October 2019 | Judicial debt cleanup procedures for individuals. First case: a debtor's debts of CNY 2.14 million were restructured. | Operational but limited scope |
| Shenzhen | March 2021 | First formal personal bankruptcy regulation in mainland China. Allows liquidation, reorganization, and reconciliation for individuals. | Over 500 cases processed. CNY 193 million in debt addressed. No individual has had debts fully discharged as of 2026. |
| ~260 county-level jurisdictions | By 2023 | Various local equivalents of personal bankruptcy | Fragmented, inconsistent rules |

**Current trajectory (2026):** In March 2026, Chinese media reported increasing pressure for a nationwide personal bankruptcy law, driven by rising consumer debt and the real estate downturn. However, no national legislation has been enacted. The pilot programs remain localized and inconsistent.

**Significance for the framework:** China's absence of personal bankruptcy is the starkest illustration of the "no exit" thesis. In a system with no discharge mechanism, financial death is permanent. The debtor cannot restart. Combined with the blacklist system, which restricts daily life, the financially dead person exists in a legal limbo that has no parallel in Western legal systems. The floor does not exist -- there is no bottom.

# SOUTH KOREA

## System Overview

South Korea operates under the Minbeop (Civil Code), enacted in 1958 and substantially amended in 1990 and 2005. The code was modeled on the Japanese Civil Code (which was itself modeled on the German BGB), creating a three-generation transmission chain: BGB -> Japanese Minpo -> Korean Minbeop.

The Korean system has undergone more radical modernization than the Japanese system, particularly in its 1990 abolition of male primogeniture and 2005 abolition of the hoju (patrilineal family headship) system. Korea permits joint custody after divorce (unlike Japan until 2026) and has established a dedicated child support enforcement agency (unlike either Japan or China).

Key structural features:

- **Codified defaults.** Statutory succession shares are precisely defined with a distinctive spouse bonus mechanism.
- **Forced heirship (yuryubun).** Directly modeled on the Japanese iryubun, which is itself modeled on the German Pflichtteil.
- **Joint custody available.** Korean courts can award joint custody after divorce, though it remains uncommon in practice.
- **Child support enforcement agency.** The 2014 Act on Enforcing and Supporting Child Support Payment created a dedicated agency.
- **Unified insolvency law.** The 2006 Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act covers both corporate and personal insolvency.

---

## Biological Death Defaults

### Inheritance (Civil Code, Book V -- Succession)

#### Order of Succession (Article 1000)

| Priority | Who | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| First | Direct descendants (children, grandchildren by representation) | Art. 1000(1)(i) |
| Second | Lineal ascendants (parents, grandparents) | Art. 1000(1)(ii) |
| Third | Siblings | Art. 1000(1)(iii) |
| Fourth | Collateral blood relatives within the 4th degree | Art. 1000(1)(iv) |

**Spouse's position (Article 1003):** The surviving spouse co-inherits alongside first- or second-order heirs. If no heirs of the first or second order exist, the spouse becomes the sole heir. This mirrors the Japanese system where the spouse occupies a parallel track.

#### Statutory Shares (Article 1009)

The base rule is equal shares among same-order heirs. The spouse then receives a **50% bonus** over a same-order heir's share:

| Family Configuration | Calculation | Spouse's Share | Each Child's Share |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spouse + 1 child | Spouse: 1.5 units; Child: 1 unit. Total: 2.5 units. | **3/5** (60%) | **2/5** (40%) |
| Spouse + 2 children | Spouse: 1.5; Child 1: 1; Child 2: 1. Total: 3.5 units. | **3/7** (~43%) | **2/7** (~29%) each |
| Spouse + 3 children | Spouse: 1.5; C1: 1; C2: 1; C3: 1. Total: 4.5 units. | **1/3** (~33%) | **2/9** (~22%) each |
| Spouse + parents (no children) | Spouse: 1.5; Parent 1: 1; Parent 2: 1. Total: 3.5 units. | **3/7** (~43%) | -- |

**Comparison to Japan:** Japan gives the spouse a fixed fraction (1/2, 2/3, or 3/4 depending on the class of blood relatives). Korea uses a ratio system where the spouse gets 1.5x a same-class heir's share, then all shares are normalized. The Korean system produces intermediate results -- the spouse gets less than under Japanese rules when co-inheriting with children, but the mechanism is more flexible.

**Example:** Decedent leaves an estate of KRW 700 million, a surviving spouse, and two children. Total units = 3.5. Spouse gets 1.5/3.5 = KRW 300 million. Each child gets 1/3.5 = KRW 200 million.

#### Yuryubun -- Compulsory Portion (Articles 1112-1118)

The yuryubun (유류분) is Korea's forced heirship mechanism, structurally identical to the Japanese iryubun and German Pflichtteil.

| Entitled Person | Yuryubun Amount | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Direct descendants (children) | **1/2** of their intestate share | Art. 1112(1) |
| Surviving spouse | **1/2** of their intestate share | Art. 1112(2) |
| Lineal ascendants (parents) | **1/3** of their intestate share | Art. 1112(3) |
| Siblings | **1/3** of their intestate share | Art. 1112(4) -- **ruled unconstitutional April 2024** |

#### 2024 Constitutional Court Ruling

On April 25, 2024, the Constitutional Court of Korea (Heonbeop Jaepanso) issued a landmark ruling on the yuryubun system:

| Holding | Effect | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| Art. 1112(4) -- siblings' yuryubun | **Unconstitutional, immediately invalid.** Siblings are no longer entitled to a compulsory portion. | Immediate |
| Art. 1112(1-3) -- descendants, spouse, ascendants | **Unconstitutional but provisionally valid** due to the absence of provisions to exclude unfilial heirs or account for caregiver contributions. | National Assembly given until December 31, 2025 to revise |
| Art. 1118 -- failure to account for contributions | **Unconstitutional.** The law's failure to allow courts to consider heirs' actual contributions to the decedent's welfare or estate was found unreasonable. | National Assembly given until December 31, 2025 to revise |

**Reasoning:**

- Modern nuclear family structures make automatic sibling inheritance unreasonable -- siblings rarely contribute to the decedent's asset accumulation.
- The absence of provisions to disqualify unfilial heirs (children or parents who neglected the decedent) violates the principle of proportionality.
- The system's failure to reward heirs who actually cared for the decedent violates fairness.

**Significance for the framework:** This ruling is moving the Korean system away from pure status-based floors (you get the yuryubun because you are a child) toward performance-based floors (your floor depends on your actual relationship with the decedent). This is philosophically closer to the Chinese model (Art. 1130's adjustment for contribution) than to the German/Japanese model. Korea is the first East Asian system to constitutionally require that caregiver contributions be factored into forced heirship.

### Abolition of Male Primogeniture and Hoju System

| Year | Reform | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1990 | Civil Code amendment | Abolished male primogeniture (hojuje) in inheritance. All children inherit equally regardless of sex or birth order. |
| 2005 | Constitutional Court ruling | Declared the hoju (patrilineal family headship) system unconstitutional. |
| 2008 | Implementation | Family Relations Register (FRR) replaced the hoju system. Women can serve as primary registrants. Children may, in some cases, take either parent's surname. |

**Comparison to Japan:** Japan eliminated primogeniture in the 1947 postwar reforms but retained the koseki system with its family-unit structure. Korea went further by entirely replacing the patrilineal registration framework.

## Parental Death: Custody After Divorce

### Legal Framework (Civil Code Art. 909)

Korean law permits joint custody after divorce, though it is not the default:

| Feature | Korean Rule |
|---|---|
| During marriage | Joint parental authority (Art. 909(1)) |
| After divorce -- agreement | Parents may agree on custody arrangement, including joint custody |
| After divorce -- court decision | Court determines custody based on best interests. Joint custody is not prohibited but is unusual. |
| Visitation rights | Non-custodial parent has visitation rights. The 2007 reform strengthened these rights. |

**Criteria for joint custody:** Korean courts apply a multi-factor test:

1. Whether both parents are prepared to share custody
2. Whether there is substantial divergence in child-rearing values
3. Whether both parents live sufficiently close to each other
4. Whether the child has the emotional capacity to adjust to shared custody

**In practice:** Joint custody remains rare. The dominant outcome is sole custody awarded to one parent (typically the mother). However, the legal option exists, which distinguishes Korea from Japan's pre-2026 system.

### Child Support Enforcement

Korea has historically had weak child support enforcement, but has taken stronger steps than Japan:

| Mechanism | Details |
|---|---|
| **Act on Enforcing and Supporting Child Support Payment** (enacted 2014, amended 2015) | Established a dedicated government agency for child support enforcement |
| **Child Support Enforcement & Management Agency** (양육비이행관리원) | Government body that assists custodial parents in collecting child support |
| **Government advance payment** (effective July 2025) | Government pays child support in advance to custodial parents when non-custodial parents fail to pay, then collects from the non-paying parent |
| **Non-payment rate** | A 2022 Gender Equality Ministry study found 80.7% of single-parent households were not receiving child support |

**Comparison across East Asia:**

| Feature | Japan | China | South Korea |
|---|---|---|---|
| Joint custody available | From April 2026 | Yes (but unusual) | Yes (but unusual) |
| Dedicated enforcement agency | No | No | Yes (since 2014) |
| Government advance payment | No | No | Yes (from 2025) |
| Non-payment rate | ~72% (estimated) | No reliable data | ~80.7% (2022) |
| Income withholding | Limited (from 2026) | Court-ordered, uneven | Available but underused |

**Significance for the framework:** All three East Asian systems share a pattern of weak child support enforcement. Even Korea, which has the strongest institutional infrastructure, reports over 80% non-payment. The floor exists in statute but collapses in practice. The child support obligation is a legal entitlement that functions more like a moral aspiration.

## Financial Death

### Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act (2006)

Korea enacted the Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act (Chaemu-ja Hoesaeng mit Pasan-e Gwanhan Beomnyul, DRBA) in 2006, consolidating four previous statutes: the Corporate Reorganization Act, the Composition Act, the Bankruptcy Act, and the Act Concerning Rehabilitation of Individual Debtors.

The DRBA covers both corporate and personal insolvency -- unlike China's Enterprise Bankruptcy Law, which covers only enterprises.

| Procedure | Purpose | Available to individuals? |
|---|---|---|
| Rehabilitation (hoesaeng) | Court-supervised reorganization with debt restructuring | Yes |
| Bankruptcy (pasan) | Liquidation with discharge of remaining debts | Yes |
| Individual rehabilitation (gein hoesaeng) | Simplified procedure for individuals with regular income | Yes -- the primary personal insolvency mechanism |

**Treatment of child support:** Korean bankruptcy law follows the general pattern of treating family support obligations as priority claims. Child support obligations are not dischargeable through bankruptcy proceedings. The specific treatment is consistent with the principle found across all three East Asian systems -- and across most civil law jurisdictions globally -- that family support obligations survive financial death.

**Comparison to Japan and China:**

| Feature | Japan | China | South Korea |
|---|---|---|---|
| Personal bankruptcy available | Yes | No (nationally) | Yes |
| Child support non-dischargeable | Yes | N/A (no personal bankruptcy) | Yes |
| Priority status for family support | Yes | N/A | Yes |

# Synthesis: The Germanic Transmission Chain

## How the BGB Reached East Asia

```
BGB (1896/1900)
  |
  v
Japanese Minpo (1896-1898) -- direct adoption of first BGB draft
  |                            + retained ie (household) system for family/succession
  |                            + 1947 reform eliminated ie, kept Germanic structure
  |
  +---> Korean Minbeop (1958) -- adopted from Japanese model during/after colonial era
  |                               + 1990: abolished primogeniture
  |                               + 2005: abolished hoju system
  |                               + 2024: Constitutional Court reformed yuryubun
  |
  +---> Chinese law (indirect, via Republican-era legal reform)
           + Soviet overlay during Mao era (1949-1978)
           + 1985 Succession Law: hybrid Germanic/Soviet
           + 2021 Civil Code: return to Germanic codification model
```

## Forced Share Comparison

| Feature | Germany (Pflichtteil) | Japan (Iryubun) | South Korea (Yuryubun) | China (Art. 1141) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Protected heirs | Children, spouse, parents | Children, spouse, parents | Children, spouse, parents, ~~siblings~~ | Only heirs who lack ability to work and income |
| Amount | 1/2 of intestate share | 1/2 of intestate share (1/3 for parents only) | 1/2 for children/spouse; 1/3 for parents; ~~1/3 for siblings~~ | "Necessary portion" (no fixed fraction) |
| Condition | Unconditional (status-based) | Unconditional (status-based) | Unconditional (status-based) -- but 2024 ruling demands reform to include performance factors | Needs-based (must demonstrate inability to work + no income) |
| Nature of claim | Monetary claim against heirs | Monetary claim (since 2018 reform) | Claim for return of infringed portion | In-kind reservation in the will |
| Can be denied? | Only on extreme grounds (BGB SS 2333) | Only on extreme grounds (Art. 892) | Currently no exclusion mechanism -- 2024 ruling demands one | Will is simply invalid to the extent it violates Art. 1141 |

## Custody After Divorce: The Parental Death Spectrum

| Feature | Germany | Japan (pre-2026) | Japan (post-2026) | South Korea | China |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Default | Joint custody continues | Sole custody mandatory | Joint or sole, by agreement/court | Sole custody typical, joint available | Sole physical custody typical |
| Joint custody | Yes (default) | **No** | Yes (available) | Yes (available but rare) | Not formally, but both parents retain obligations |
| Visitation rights | Legally enforceable | Weak, often denied | Strengthened (in theory) | Statutory right, weak enforcement | Statutory right (Art. 1086) |
| Enforcement | Contempt, fines, coercion | Essentially none | New seizure provisions | Slow multi-step process | Court fines, detention |
| Structural equivalence to death | No -- non-custodial parent retains full rights | **Yes** -- non-custodial parent legally erased | Reduced -- depends on court outcomes | Partial -- legal rights exist but enforcement gaps | Partial -- rights exist on paper |

## The Key Finding

Japan's pre-2026 sole-custody rule (old Article 819) was the purest example of "parental death by operation of law" in any developed legal system. When a Japanese couple divorced:

1. The koseki was modified to reflect sole parental authority in one parent.
2. The other parent lost all legal authority over the child.
3. No enforceable visitation right existed.
4. Child support enforcement was negligible.

The non-custodial parent was, for all legal purposes, dead to the child. The child's legal family structure was identical to what it would be if that parent had biologically died. This is not a metaphor -- it is a structural description of the legal outcome.

The April 2026 reform theoretically ends this pattern, but cultural inertia, weak enforcement, and the DV exception create significant uncertainty about whether the structural outcome will actually change in practice.

## The "No Exit" Axis: Financial Death Across East Asia

| Country | Personal bankruptcy available? | Discharge available? | Effect on individual |
|---|---|---|---|
| Japan | Yes | Yes | Financial death is temporary. Restart possible. |
| South Korea | Yes | Yes | Financial death is temporary. Restart possible. |
| China | No (nationally) | No | **Financial death is permanent.** No discharge. No restart. Blacklist system imposes ongoing restrictions. |

China's absence of a national personal bankruptcy system creates a unique "no exit" condition. A financially dead person in China cannot become financially alive again. The debts persist indefinitely, the blacklist restrictions continue, and there is no legal mechanism for a fresh start. The pilot programs in Shenzhen and Wenzhou are beginning to address this, but as of 2026, no individual has successfully obtained a full debt discharge even under the Shenzhen pilot.

This is the inverse of the inheritance floor. The Pflichtteil/iryubun/yuryubun provides a floor -- you cannot be reduced below a minimum share. Personal bankruptcy provides a different kind of floor -- you cannot be reduced below a minimum standard of living. China lacks the second floor entirely.
