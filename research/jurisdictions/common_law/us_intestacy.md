# United States — Death, Default, and Floor

## System Overview

The United States operates three parallel models for intestate succession (what happens when someone dies without a will), each producing different default distributions:

1. **Uniform Probate Code (UPC) states** — 16 states have adopted the UPC in its entirety (Alaska, Arizona, Colorado, Florida, Hawaii, Idaho, Maine, Michigan, Minnesota, Montana, Nebraska, New Mexico, North Dakota, South Carolina, South Dakota, Utah). The UPC provides a graduated spousal share that varies based on whether descendants are shared with the surviving spouse. Under UPC § 2-102, the spouse's share ranges from the entire estate (no descendants, no parents) down to $150,000 + 50% of the balance (descendants not of the surviving spouse). UPC § 2-103 distributes the remainder to descendants by representation.

2. **Community property states** — 9 states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin). Property acquired during marriage is presumed jointly owned 50/50. At death, the surviving spouse automatically retains their half of community property. The decedent's half of community property, plus all separate property, passes through intestacy rules. The surviving spouse's share of the decedent's separate property varies: California gives 1/3 if there are multiple children (Cal. Prob. Code § 6401); Texas gives 1/3 of separate personalty and a life estate in 1/3 of separate realty (Tex. Estates Code § 201.002).

3. **Common law / separate property states** — the remaining ~25 non-UPC, non-community-property states. These use their own intestacy statutes, typically giving the surviving spouse a preferential share (first $X plus a fraction) with the remainder to descendants. Examples: New York gives spouse first $50,000 + 50% of balance (N.Y. Est. Powers & Trusts Law § 4-1.1); Pennsylvania gives spouse first $30,000 + 50% of balance (20 Pa. C.S. § 2102).

**Federal vs. state family law:** Family law (marriage, divorce, custody, child support) is primarily state law. However, the federal government has overlaid a mandatory child support enforcement infrastructure through Title IV-D of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. §§ 651-669b), enacted in 1975. This federal overlay creates a system in which states receive 66% federal reimbursement for enforcement costs, creating a direct financial incentive for aggressive enforcement — regardless of the obligor's ability to pay.

**Constitutional floor:** The U.S. Supreme Court has established a constitutional floor for parental rights through the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment: parents have a "fundamental liberty interest" in the care, custody, and control of their children (Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)). The state cannot sever this interest without meeting specific procedural and evidentiary requirements.

## The Title IV-D Incentive Structure

Under 42 U.S.C. § 655, the federal government reimburses states for 66% of their administrative costs for operating child support enforcement programs. This means for every $3 a state spends on enforcement, the federal government pays $2.

In addition to the base cost reimbursement, 42 U.S.C. § 658a creates a separate incentive payment pool. States are paid incentive bonuses based on five performance measures: (1) paternity establishment percentage; (2) support order establishment; (3) current collections rate; (4) arrearage collections rate; (5) cost-effectiveness ratio. The incentive payments are a function of performance — the more a state collects, the more federal incentive money it receives.

The combined effect is that states face a structural financial incentive to maximize enforcement activity:

- **Cost side:** The state pays only 34 cents per dollar of enforcement cost. The marginal cost of pursuing an additional obligor is 34% of actual cost.
- **Revenue side:** Collections generate direct revenue (in former TANF cases, the state keeps a portion of collections to reimburse itself for cash assistance paid). Performance-based incentive payments under § 658a reward states for high collection rates.
- **Perverse outcome:** The system profits from the existence of obligors in arrearage. An obligor who pays on time generates less enforcement activity (and therefore less federal reimbursement and fewer incentive payments) than an obligor who falls behind and triggers the full enforcement cascade. The system has no equivalent incentive to verify that the underlying support order is calibrated to the obligor's actual ability to pay.

The enforcement cascade under 42 U.S.C. § 666 is mandatory — states must implement these procedures as a condition of receiving Title IV-D funding:

| Enforcement Mechanism | Statutory Basis | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Automatic income withholding | § 666(a)(1)-(2) | Any support order (no arrearage required) |
| Tax refund intercept (federal and state) | § 664 | Arrearage exists |
| Credit bureau reporting | § 666(a)(7) | Arrearage reported |
| Driver's license suspension | § 666(a)(16) | Arrearage; state-determined threshold |
| Professional/occupational license suspension | § 666(a)(16) | Arrearage; state-determined threshold |
| Passport denial | § 652(k) | Arrearage exceeds $2,500 |
| Financial institution data match (asset seizure) | § 666(a)(17) | Arrearage exists |
| Federal criminal prosecution | 18 U.S.C. § 228 | Willful failure to pay; $5,000+ arrearage or 1+ year unpaid; interstate |
| Civil contempt / incarceration | State law, subject to Turner | Court discretion; ability-to-pay finding required |

None of these mechanisms have a corresponding automatic review of whether the underlying order remains calibrated to the obligor's current income. Modification requires the obligor to file a motion and prove a "substantial change in circumstances" — an affirmative burden placed on the person already subject to the enforcement cascade.

## Anonymized Parallel

A parent signs an allocation judgment prepared by the other party's counsel. The document is presented during a brief court appearance. The signing parent does not have counsel. No independent review of the terms occurs. No fitness hearing is conducted — fitness is not at issue because the form of the proceeding is "agreed" allocation, not contested termination.

The judgment awards sole decision-making authority to one parent. Parenting time for the other parent is listed as "reserved" — meaning zero scheduled time, to be determined at a future date. The system processes this as consent.

The defaults now activate. The parent with reserved time becomes the noncustodial parent. A child support order enters based on the financial affidavit submitted by the custodial parent. Income withholding begins within two business days. The Title IV-D machinery engages.

If the noncustodial parent's economic circumstances change — job loss, illness, reduced income — the support order does not automatically adjust. The obligation accrues at the original rate. Interest may accrue on the arrearage (state-dependent). The enforcement cascade activates: tax refund intercept, credit reporting, license suspension, passport denial.

If the noncustodial parent files for bankruptcy, the arrearage is nondischargeable. 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(5). If they seek modification, they bear the burden of proving a substantial change in circumstances while simultaneously being subject to contempt proceedings for the existing arrearage. If they are held in contempt, they may be incarcerated — even though Turner v. Rogers requires an ability-to-pay finding, compliance with the four safeguards is inconsistent.

The constitutional floor — the fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and control of one's children — was never tested. No unfitness finding was ever made (Stanley). No clear and convincing evidence standard was ever applied (Santosky). No court ever weighed the parent's interest against the state's interest (Troxel). The parent's signature on the allocation judgment bypassed every constitutional protection that exists for contested proceedings.

The result is a system designed for deadbeat parents applied to a parent who signed a document. The enforcement mechanisms were built to recoup welfare expenditures from absent parents (the original 1975 purpose of Title IV-D). They are now applied to a parent whose absence was created by the allocation judgment itself. There is no exit mechanism — unlike every other debt obligation in American law, domestic support obligations cannot be discharged in bankruptcy, cannot be reduced retroactively, and accumulate continuously regardless of ability to pay.

The defaults chose for this person. The defaults were not written for this person. The defaults were written for the state.
