# France -- Death, Default, and Floor

## System Overview

France operates under the **Code Civil** (1804, Napoleon), a codified civil law system that provides the strongest children's inheritance protection in any Western legal system. France is a founding signatory of the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR), ratified in 1974, making ECHR Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life) directly enforceable in French courts. The French legal system is structured around the **bloc de constitutionnalite**, which includes the 1958 Constitution, the 1789 Declaration of the Rights of Man, the Preamble to the 1946 Constitution, and the 2004 Environmental Charter -- all of which have full constitutional force.

The system is built on a principle foreign to American common law: **children have rights that cannot be contracted away by their parents.** The reserve hereditaire (forced share) ensures every child inherits. Joint parental authority survives separation by default. Non-payment of child support is a criminal offense. And the ECHR provides an external floor that no domestic legislature can breach.

---

## Biological Death Defaults (Succession ab intestat)

When a person dies without a will, the Code Civil provides automatic distribution rules. The surviving spouse's rights were substantially strengthened by the **Loi n 2001-1135 du 3 decembre 2001** (effective July 1, 2002), which elevated the spouse from "poor relation" of inheritance law to a protected heir.

### With Children Present

| Heirs | Spouse's Share | Children's Share | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Children (all from the marriage) + spouse | Spouse **chooses**: usufruct of 100% of estate **OR** 1/4 in full ownership | Remaining 3/4 (or bare ownership subject to usufruct) divided equally among children | Code Civil Art. 757 |
| Children (one or more NOT from the marriage) + spouse | Spouse receives 1/4 in full ownership (no usufruct option) | Remaining 3/4 divided equally among all children | Code Civil Art. 757 |

**Key detail:** When all children are common children of both spouses, the surviving spouse has a **choice** (option) between usufruct of the entirety or 1/4 ownership. If the spouse does not declare within three months of being asked by the other heirs, the spouse is deemed to have chosen usufruct (Art. 758-1). When any child is not from the marriage, the spouse gets only 1/4 ownership -- no usufruct option. This protects children from a second marriage from being dispossessed.

### Without Children

| Heirs | Spouse's Share | Others' Share | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spouse + both parents of deceased | 1/2 of estate | 1/4 to each parent | Code Civil Art. 757-1 |
| Spouse + one surviving parent | 3/4 of estate | 1/4 to surviving parent | Code Civil Art. 757-1 |
| Spouse alone (no children, no parents) | **Entire estate** | Nothing | Code Civil Art. 757-2 |

**Additional spouse protections:**
- **Right of habitation** (droit viager au logement): The surviving spouse has a right to remain in the principal family residence for life, with use of its furnishings (Art. 764). This right is an automatic legal entitlement, not dependent on testamentary disposition.
- **Tax exemption**: Since the law of August 21, 2007, the surviving spouse is completely exempt from inheritance tax.

## The Reserve Hereditaire -- Children Cannot Be Disinherited

This is the core structural difference between French and Anglo-American inheritance law. Articles 912-930 of the Code Civil establish that a portion of every estate is **reserved by law** for the children. The testator may only freely dispose of the remainder (the **quotite disponible**).

### Reserved Share Table

| Number of Children | Reserved Share (Reserve) | Disposable Portion (Quotite Disponible) | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 child | **1/2** (50%) of estate | 1/2 (50%) | Code Civil Art. 913 |
| 2 children | **2/3** (66.67%) of estate | 1/3 (33.33%) | Code Civil Art. 913 |
| 3 or more children | **3/4** (75%) of estate | 1/4 (25%) | Code Civil Art. 913 |

### Definitions (Art. 912)

- **Reserve hereditaire**: "La part des biens et droits successoraux dont la loi assure la devolution libre de charges a certains heritiers dits reservataires, s'ils sont appeles a la succession et l'acceptent." (The portion of estate property and succession rights which the law ensures will pass free of charges to certain heirs called reservataires, if they are called to the succession and accept it.)
- **Quotite disponible**: "La part des biens et droits successoraux qui n'est pas reservee par la loi et dont le defunt a pu disposer librement par des liberalites." (The portion of estate property and succession rights not reserved by law, which the deceased was able to freely dispose of by gifts.)

### Why This Matters

Napoleon designed this system with a specific purpose: **to prevent dynastic concentration of wealth and ensure equal division among children at each generation.** The Revolutionary and Napoleonic legislators raised the reserve hereditaire to a high level precisely to level out wealth by dividing it at each generation. This was an explicitly anti-aristocratic, anti-primogeniture measure -- a direct repudiation of the feudal system abolished on August 4, 1789.

The reserve hereditaire **cannot be overridden by will.** If a testator's gifts (inter vivos or testamentary) exceed the quotite disponible, the reserved heirs have an **action en reduction** (action to reduce excessive gifts) to claw back the excess (Art. 920 et seq.). This action survives the death and can be exercised by the children against any beneficiary who received more than the disposable portion.

### International Protection (Art. 913 al. 3, added by Loi n 2021-1109)

When the deceased or at least one of their children is, at the time of death, a national of an EU Member State or habitually resident there, and when the applicable foreign law provides no protective mechanism for children, each child (or their heirs or successors) may exercise a **compensatory levy** (prelevement compensatoire) on assets located in France to restore the reserved rights that French law would have granted. This provision was specifically designed to prevent forum-shopping to circumvent forced heirship.

### Comparison with the United States

In 49 of 50 US states, a parent can **completely disinherit** their children by will. The sole exception is **Louisiana**, which inherited the French model directly through the Napoleonic Code. Louisiana Civil Code Art. 1493 et seq. provides forced heirship, but only for children under 24 or permanently disabled -- a significantly weaker protection than the French system, which applies to all children regardless of age.

## Parental Authority Defaults (Autorite Parentale)

French family law underwent three major reforms that progressively established full co-parenting as the default:

| Reform | Date | What Changed |
|---|---|---|
| Loi n 70-459 | June 4, 1970 | **Puissance paternelle** (paternal power) abolished. Replaced by **autorite parentale** (parental authority) exercised jointly by both parents. |
| Loi n 93-22 | January 8, 1993 | Joint parental authority becomes the **rule** for all children regardless of filiation (legitimate or natural). In divorce, joint exercise of authority is the default; sole authority requires justification in the child's interest. |
| Loi n 2002-305 | March 4, 2002 | Principles of **coparentalite** (co-parenting) and **residence alternee** (alternating residence) codified. "It is in the child's interest to be raised by both parents." |

### Current Default Rules

| Event | Default | Override Requires | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Child is born to married parents | Joint parental authority | N/A | Code Civil Art. 372 |
| Child is born to unmarried parents (both recognized) | Joint parental authority | N/A | Code Civil Art. 372 |
| Parents separate or divorce | **Joint parental authority continues** | N/A -- separation has no effect on parental authority | Code Civil Art. 373-2 |
| Residence after separation | Court may order **residence alternee** (alternating residence) at each parent's home | Agreement of parents or court order | Code Civil Art. 373-2-9 |
| One parent seeks sole authority | Must demonstrate **serious grounds** (motifs graves) | Judicial finding that child's interest requires it | Code Civil Art. 373-2-1 |
| Parent changes residence | Must give **timely advance notice** (information prealable et en temps utile) to the other parent | Failure to notify allows other parent to seize the family judge | Code Civil Art. 373-2 |

### Key Principle: Art. 373-2

> "La separation des parents est sans incidence sur les regles de devolution de l'exercice de l'autorite parentale."

(The separation of parents has no effect on the rules governing the devolution of the exercise of parental authority.)

Each parent must **maintain personal relations** with the child and **respect the child's ties** with the other parent. This is a statutory obligation, not a discretionary standard.

## Financial Defaults (Obligation Alimentaire and Pension Alimentaire)

### The Duty to Support

| Obligation | Who Owes | To Whom | Duration | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nourish, maintain, and raise children | Both parents jointly | Their children | Until child can meet own needs (continues past majority for students, disabled) | Code Civil Art. 203 (married), Art. 371-2 (all parents) |
| Contribution proportional to resources | Each parent | Children, in proportion to resources of both parents and needs of the child | Ongoing | Code Civil Art. 371-2 |

### Enforcement Mechanisms for Non-Payment

France has a **layered enforcement system** for unpaid pension alimentaire:

| Mechanism | How It Works | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Paiement direct** (direct payment) | Creditor, through a huissier (bailiff/commissaire de justice), obtains payment directly from the debtor's employer, bank, or any third party holding sums owed to the debtor. Recovers arrears from the **prior 6 months** plus all future installments. | Code des procedures civiles d'execution Art. L213-1 et seq. |
| **Saisie sur salaire** (wage garnishment) | Court-ordered deduction from debtor's wages | Code du travail Art. L3252-1 et seq. |
| **Recouvrement par le Tresor public** (Treasury recovery) | When private enforcement fails, the Public Prosecutor can refer the matter to the Treasury, which recovers the debt using **tax collection powers** (the same powers used to collect taxes) | Loi n 75-618 du 11 juillet 1975 |
| **Abandon de famille** (criminal prosecution) | **Criminal offense**: 2 years imprisonment + 15,000 EUR fine for non-payment exceeding 2 months | Code Penal Art. 227-3 |
| **Failure to notify address change** | Criminal offense: 6 months imprisonment + 7,500 EUR fine for failing to notify creditor of address change within 1 month | Code Penal Art. 227-4 |

### The Criminal Dimension: Abandon de Famille

Code Penal Art. 227-3:

> "Le fait, pour une personne, de ne pas executer une decision judiciaire ou une convention judiciairement homologuee lui imposant de verser au profit d'un enfant mineur [...] une pension, une contribution, des subsides ou toute autre prestation due en vertu d'une obligation familiale prevue par le Code civil, en demeurant plus de deux mois sans s'en acquitter integralement, est puni de deux ans d'emprisonnement et de 15 000 euros d'amende."

Key elements:
- The offense is constituted after **two months** of non-payment
- The debtor who claims inability to pay bears the **burden of proving** their own impecuniosity (the prosecution does not need to prove ability to pay)
- Statute of limitations: **3 years** from the offense
- This is a **delit** (intermediate criminal offense), not a mere contravention

## Insolvency and Child Support (Surendettement des Particuliers)

The French personal over-indebtedness procedure is governed by the **Code de la consommation, Livre VII** (Articles L711-1 to L771-12). It provides debt relief to individuals in a situation of "manifest impossibility of meeting all debts, professional and non-professional, due and to come" (Art. L711-1).

### Does Child Support Survive Insolvency?

| Question | Answer | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Can pension alimentaire be rescheduled? | **No.** Alimentary debts are explicitly excluded from imposed rescheduling measures. | Code de la consommation Art. L733-1 (dettes autres que les dettes alimentaires) |
| Can pension alimentaire be suspended? | **No.** Suspension of payments for up to 2 years applies only to "creditors other than alimentary debts." | Code de la consommation Art. L733-1 |
| Can pension alimentaire be erased/forgiven? | **No.** Alimentary debts cannot be erased or reduced without the **express agreement** of the creditor. | Code de la consommation Art. L733-7 |
| Can enforcement continue during the procedure? | **Yes.** Execution procedures (paiement direct, saisie sur salaire) for alimentary debts continue even while other debts are stayed. | Code des procedures civiles d'execution |
| Does bankruptcy protect the debtor? | **No.** Even in a retablissement personnel (personal recovery -- the French equivalent of Chapter 7), alimentary debts survive. | Code de la consommation |

**Summary:** In French law, child support obligations are **absolutely privileged debts** (creances privilegiees). They survive every form of insolvency procedure. The debtor cannot discharge them through any bankruptcy-like mechanism. The only way to modify them is to return to the family court and demonstrate a material change in circumstances -- the insolvency commission has no jurisdiction over alimentary debts.

## Constitutional and Supranational Protection

### The Bloc de Constitutionnalite

France's constitutional order includes multiple sources with full constitutional force:

| Source | Key Family Protection | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Preamble to the 1946 Constitution, alinea 10** | "La Nation assure a l'individu et a la famille les conditions necessaires a leur developpement." (The Nation ensures to the individual and the family the conditions necessary for their development.) | Constitutional obligation on the State to implement a policy of national solidarity in favor of families. The Conseil constitutionnel (Constitutional Council) uses this to review legislation for constitutional compliance. |
| **1789 Declaration of the Rights of Man, Art. 17** | Property rights | Protects the inheritance rights established by the reserve hereditaire |
| **ECHR Art. 8** (Convention europeenne des droits de l'homme) | "Everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life, his home and his correspondence." | France ratified in 1974. Directly enforceable in French courts. The Cour de cassation and Conseil d'Etat apply ECHR Art. 8 to family law disputes. |

### Convergence of Constitutional and Convention Protections

The Conseil constitutionnel has recognized that the right to lead a normal family life (droit de mener une vie familiale normale), derived from the 1946 Preamble's alinea 10, is functionally equivalent to ECHR Art. 8. Three common rights are protected by both the constitutional bloc and the ECHR:

1. **Freedom / right to marry** (liberte du mariage)
2. **Right to respect for family life** (droit au respect de la vie familiale)
3. **Right to respect for private life** (droit au respect de la vie privee)

This dual protection means that French family law is constrained both from above (by the Constitution, reviewed by the Conseil constitutionnel) and from outside (by the ECHR, enforced by the European Court of Human Rights in Strasbourg). A French law that violated children's fundamental rights could be struck down by either body.

## Forced Heirship / Floor Mechanisms

| Floor | Mechanism | Can It Be Breached? | What Happens If Breached |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reserve hereditaire** | 50-75% of estate reserved for children (Art. 913) | No. Constitutional principle. International clawback provision (Art. 913 al. 3). | Children exercise **action en reduction** to claw back excessive gifts (Art. 920 et seq.) |
| **Joint parental authority** | Continues after separation (Art. 373-2) | Only by judicial finding of **serious grounds** (Art. 373-2-1) | Court restores joint authority absent continued justification |
| **Pension alimentaire** | Each parent contributes proportionally (Art. 371-2) | Non-payment is a **criminal offense** (Code Penal Art. 227-3) | 2 years imprisonment + 15,000 EUR fine; Treasury collection powers |
| **Child support in insolvency** | Alimentary debts survive all insolvency procedures | No bankruptcy-like mechanism can discharge them | Enforcement continues; insolvency commission has no jurisdiction |
| **ECHR Art. 8** | Right to respect for family life | France is bound as signatory; enforceable by ECtHR | Adverse judgment from Strasbourg; France must amend its law |
| **1946 Preamble, alinea 10** | State must ensure conditions for family development | Reviewed by Conseil constitutionnel | Unconstitutional law is struck down |

## Who Wrote the Defaults?

| Default | Author | Date | Whose Interest | Design Logic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Reserve hereditaire** | Napoleon / Assemblees revolutionnaires / Portalis, Tronchet, Bigot de Preameneu, Maleville (drafters of the Code Civil) | 1804 (roots in Revolutionary legislation of 1791-1794) | Children (anti-dynastic, anti-primogeniture) | Prevent concentration of wealth in one heir; force division at each generation; destroy feudal aristocratic succession |
| **Joint parental authority** | Parliament (Assemblee nationale) | 1970 (Loi n 70-459) / 1993 (Loi n 93-22) / 2002 (Loi n 2002-305) | Children (right to be raised by both parents) | Replace paternal power with co-parenting; separation does not sever the parent-child bond |
| **Pension alimentaire enforcement** | Parliament | Code Penal (criminal); Loi du 11 juillet 1975 (Treasury recovery) | Children and custodial parent | Non-payment is not a civil matter to be litigated -- it is a crime to be prosecuted |
| **Insolvency protection** | Parliament | Code de la consommation, Livre VII | Children (alimentary creditors) | Child support cannot be sacrificed to commercial creditors; children come first in the priority stack |
| **Constitutional family protection** | Constituent Assembly of the Fourth Republic | 1946 (Preamble, alinea 10) | Family unit | State has an affirmative obligation to ensure conditions for family development |
| **ECHR Art. 8** | Council of Europe | 1950 (ratified by France 1974) | Individual and family | Supranational floor: no member state can legislate away the right to family life |

## Anonymized Parallel

In French law, children's rights are structural -- they are built into the architecture of the system at every level: inheritance, parental authority, financial support, insolvency, constitutional law, and supranational human rights law.

A parent cannot disinherit a child. Parental authority continues jointly after separation as an automatic default. Non-payment of child support for more than two months is a criminal offense (abandon de famille), punishable by two years imprisonment -- and the burden falls on the debtor to prove they cannot pay, not on the state to prove they can. Even if a parent declares personal bankruptcy, child support obligations survive intact; the insolvency commission has no power to reduce, reschedule, or erase them.

The system was designed this way deliberately. Napoleon's drafters built the reserve hereditaire to destroy aristocratic primogeniture -- to ensure that wealth divides at each generation rather than concentrating. The parental authority reforms of 1970, 1993, and 2002 progressively dismantled paternal power and replaced it with a principle that "it is in the child's interest to be raised by both parents." The criminal enforcement of child support was designed to make default a prosecutable act, not merely a debt to be collected.

But the enforcement mechanism contains the same structural tension found in US contempt proceedings. When a parent cannot pay and is imprisoned for abandon de famille, they are even less able to earn income to meet the obligation. The two-year prison term under Code Penal Art. 227-3 creates the same coercive paradox as incarceration for civil contempt in American family courts: the purge condition (pay the support) becomes impossible to satisfy from inside a cell.

The critical difference is that France has two external floors that US family law lacks:

1. **The ECHR (Article 8)**: The European Court of Human Rights in Strasbourg provides a supranational check on French domestic law. A parent who believes their right to family life has been violated can bring a case to Strasbourg after exhausting domestic remedies. The ECtHR has repeatedly held that states have positive obligations to facilitate family reunification and parent-child contact. No equivalent external tribunal exists for US family law.

2. **The reserve hereditaire**: Even if a parent is completely excluded from a child's life during their lifetime, the child's inheritance rights are constitutionally protected. Death resets the ledger. The child cannot be written out of the will. The parent's estate must flow to the child regardless of the state of the relationship. In 49 of 50 US states, a disinherited child has no recourse.

The French system is not perfect. Enforcement delays, judicial discretion in residence determinations, and the practical difficulties of criminal prosecution for abandon de famille all create gaps between the law on paper and the law in practice. But the architecture is fundamentally different from the American model: the defaults protect children, the floors cannot be contracted away, and both the Constitution and an external treaty provide structural safeguards that survive any individual judge's discretion.
