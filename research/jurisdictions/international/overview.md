# International Human Rights Frameworks: Parental Rights and Family Law

## Comparative Law Analysis: The Supranational Floor

This document surveys the international treaties and case law that establish
minimum standards ("floors") for how domestic legal systems may treat parents,
children, and families. These frameworks constrain state action --- a domestic
system that falls below these norms is in violation of binding international
obligations (for state parties).

---

## 1. European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)

### Article 8 --- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life

**Full text:**

> **1.** Everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life,
> his home and his correspondence.
>
> **2.** There shall be no interference by a public authority with the
> exercise of this right except such as is in accordance with the law and is
> necessary in a democratic society in the interests of national security,
> public safety or the economic wellbeing of the country, for the prevention
> of disorder or crime, for the protection of health or morals, or for the
> protection of the rights and freedoms of others.

Article 8 applies to all 46 member states of the Council of Europe. The
European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) enforces it through binding judgments.
It is the primary mechanism through which European parents challenge state
interference with custody, contact, and family life.

#### Standard of Review

The ECtHR applies a three-part test to Article 8(2) interferences:

1. **Legality** --- the interference must be "in accordance with the law"
2. **Legitimate aim** --- the interference must pursue one of the enumerated
   aims (national security, public safety, economic wellbeing, prevention of
   disorder or crime, protection of health or morals, protection of rights
   and freedoms of others)
3. **Necessity** --- the interference must be "necessary in a democratic
   society," meaning it must respond to a "pressing social need" and be
   proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued

The Court also applies the **margin of appreciation** doctrine, allowing
states some latitude in how they implement Convention rights, but this
margin narrows where fundamental aspects of family life are at stake.

#### Key ECtHR Cases on Parental Rights

**Johansen v. Norway (1996)**
- Application No. 17383/90, judgment 7 August 1996
- **Facts:** Mother's daughter was taken into public care shortly after birth.
  Authorities permanently removed the daughter and terminated the mother's
  parental rights and access.
- **Holding:** The taking into care did not violate Article 8, but the
  permanent deprivation of parental rights and access did violate Article 8.
- **Key principle:** Taking a child into care should normally be regarded as
  a *temporary* measure, and its implementation should be consistent with the
  ultimate aim of reuniting the natural parent and the child. Permanent
  measures (deprivation of parental rights, denial of contact) require
  "particularly weighty reasons" and can only be justified if motivated by
  an "overriding requirement pertaining to the child's best interests."
- **Citation:** *Johansen v. Norway* [1996] ECHR 31

**Gorgulu v. Germany (2004)**
- Application No. 74969/01, judgment 26 February 2004
- **Facts:** A father's paternity was judicially established. The mother had
  agreed to the child's adoption immediately after birth. The father sought
  custody and access rights, which German courts denied.
- **Holding:** Violation of Article 8 with regard to refusal of custody and
  access. No violation with regard to involvement in the proceedings.
- **Key principle:** A biological father who has established paternity has
  a right under Article 8 to custody or, at minimum, access. The denial of
  both custody and contact to a father who has demonstrated genuine interest
  requires compelling justification.
- **Damages:** EUR 15,000 plus EUR 1,500 costs
- **Citation:** *Gorgulu v. Germany*, No. 74969/01 (ECtHR, 26 Feb. 2004)

**Neulinger and Shuruk v. Switzerland (2010)**
- Application No. 41615/07, Grand Chamber judgment 6 July 2010
- **Facts:** A Swiss-Israeli couple separated. The mother took the child from
  Israel to Switzerland. Israeli courts determined the child's habitual
  residence was Israel. Swiss courts ordered return under the Hague
  Abduction Convention.
- **Holding:** The Grand Chamber held that Article 8 of the ECHR takes
  precedence over the Hague Abduction Convention. The domestic court must
  ensure that application of the Hague Convention secures Article 8 rights.
  Several years had elapsed since the abduction (partly through litigation),
  and the child was well-settled in Switzerland. Return was not ordered.
- **Key principle:** When applying the Hague Convention, domestic courts must
  conduct an *in-depth examination* of the family situation and consider
  the child's best interests at the present time, not merely at the time
  of abduction. The passage of time can change the proportionality calculus.
- **Citation:** *Neulinger and Shuruk v. Switzerland* [GC], No. 41615/07
  (ECtHR, 6 July 2010)

**Strand Lobben and Others v. Norway (2019)**
- Application No. 37283/13, Grand Chamber judgment 10 September 2019
- **Facts:** A child was placed in foster care. Authorities later authorized
  adoption by the foster parents, terminating the mother's parental rights,
  without obtaining up-to-date expert evidence on the mother's current
  capacity to provide care.
- **Holding:** The Grand Chamber (reversing the Chamber judgment of
  30 November 2017) found a violation of Article 8 by a majority.
- **Key principle:** The decision-making process leading to adoption must be
  procedurally rigorous. Adoption without up-to-date expert assessment of
  the parent's current capacity violates Article 8. The passage of time
  between placement and adoption decision cannot substitute for proper
  evaluation of whether the parent has improved.
- **Significance:** This case triggered a wave of follow-on cases against
  Norway's child welfare system (Barnevernet), with the ECtHR finding
  violations in multiple subsequent cases.
- **Citation:** *Strand Lobben and Others v. Norway* [GC], No. 37283/13
  (ECtHR, 10 Sept. 2019)

#### Article 8 and Child Support Enforcement

The ECtHR has not directly ruled on imprisonment for non-payment of child
support as a standalone Article 8 issue. However, the Court has established
that:

- States have **positive obligations** under Article 8 to facilitate contact
  between parents and children after separation
- Ineffective and delayed conduct of custody and contact proceedings may
  breach positive obligations under Article 8
- Courts must exercise "exceptional diligence" in family cases, given the
  risk that passage of time may result in a de facto determination
- The lack of cooperation between separated parents does not relieve
  authorities of their positive obligations

Key cases on enforcement of contact rights include *P.K. v. Poland*,
*Ribic v. Croatia*, *Suss v. Germany*, and *S.I. v. Slovenia*, establishing
that state failure to enforce contact orders constitutes an Article 8
violation.

#### Article 8 and the Right to Contact After Separation

The ECtHR treats the right to maintain contact with one's children as a
core component of "family life" under Article 8. The state bears a positive
obligation to take measures facilitating reunion and contact. Where
authorities fail to act quickly and effectively to restore a parent's
enjoyment of visitation rights, the state may be found responsible for a
violation of Article 8.

### Article 6 --- Right to a Fair Trial

**Full text (Paragraph 1):**

> In the determination of his civil rights and obligations or of any criminal
> charge against him, everyone is entitled to a fair and public hearing within
> a reasonable time by an independent and impartial tribunal established by law.
> Judgment shall be pronounced publicly but the press and public may be excluded
> from all or part of the trial in the interest of morals, public order or
> national security in a democratic society, where the interests of juveniles
> or the protection of the private life of the parties so require, or to the
> extent strictly necessary in the opinion of the court in special circumstances
> where publicity would prejudice the interests of justice.

#### Application to Family Law Proceedings

Family law proceedings --- including custody, access, child support, and
divorce --- fall within the scope of "civil rights and obligations" under
Article 6(1). Every party to a family law case is entitled to:

- A **fair hearing** --- each party must be given all relevant information
  and evidence, and each party must have the opportunity to present their case
- A hearing within a **reasonable time** --- prolonged delay in family
  proceedings can itself constitute a violation
- An **independent and impartial tribunal** established by law
- A right to an **explanation** of how the court reached its decision

#### Right to Legal Representation

Article 6 does not create an automatic right to free legal representation in
civil cases. However, the ECtHR has held that where a case is sufficiently
complex that a party cannot effectively represent themselves, failure to
provide legal aid may breach Article 6. This has particular implications for
self-represented litigants in family courts facing well-represented opposing
parties.

### Protocol 4, Article 1 --- Prohibition of Imprisonment for Debt

**Full text:**

> No one shall be deprived of his liberty merely on the ground of inability
> to fulfil a contractual obligation.

#### Ratification Status

Protocol 4 has been ratified by **42 of the 46** Council of Europe member
states. The four non-ratifying states are:

- **Greece** --- signed but not ratified
- **Switzerland** --- signed but not ratified
- **Turkey** --- signed but not ratified
- **United Kingdom** --- not signed, not ratified

#### Interaction with Child Support Enforcement

The word "merely" is critical: Protocol 4, Article 1 does not prohibit
imprisonment where there is an additional element such as fraud, contempt
of court, or willful refusal. What it prohibits is imprisonment *solely*
on the ground of inability to pay a contractual debt.

The question of whether child support constitutes a "contractual obligation"
under Protocol 4 is significant. Child support obligations typically arise
from statute or court order, not from contract. This creates an argument
that Protocol 4's protection does not extend to court-ordered child support.
However, the underlying norm --- that inability to pay should not result in
deprivation of liberty --- aligns with ICCPR Article 11 (below).

## 2. International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)

The ICCPR was adopted by the UN General Assembly on 16 December 1966 and
entered into force on 23 March 1976. As of 2025, it has **175 state parties**
and 6 additional signatories (including China and Cuba) who have not ratified.
The United States ratified the ICCPR in 1992.

The Human Rights Committee (HRC) monitors compliance and issues General
Comments interpreting the Covenant's provisions.

### Article 23 --- Protection of the Family

**Full text:**

> **1.** The family is the natural and fundamental group unit of society and is
> entitled to protection by society and the State.
>
> **2.** The right of men and women of marriageable age to marry and to found
> a family shall be recognized.
>
> **3.** No marriage shall be entered into without the free and full consent
> of the intending spouses.
>
> **4.** States Parties to the present Covenant shall take appropriate steps
> to ensure equality of rights and responsibilities of spouses as to marriage,
> during marriage and at its dissolution. In the case of dissolution, provision
> shall be made for the necessary protection of any children.

#### Human Rights Committee Interpretation

**General Comment No. 19 (1990)** provides the authoritative interpretation:

- The concept of "family" may differ from state to state and even within
  regions of a state; no single standard definition is imposed
- When a group of persons is regarded as a family under the legislation and
  practice of a state, it *must* be given the protection referred to in
  Article 23
- States must ensure equality of rights and responsibilities between spouses
  during marriage and at its dissolution
- At dissolution, provision must be made for the "necessary protection of
  any children" --- this is an affirmative obligation on the state

#### Significance

Article 23(4) creates a dual obligation at dissolution: (a) equality between
spouses, and (b) protection of children. These two requirements can conflict
when a system systematically burdens one spouse while purporting to protect
children.

### Article 24 --- Rights of the Child

**Full text:**

> **1.** Every child shall have, without any discrimination as to race, colour,
> sex, language, religion, national or social origin, property or birth, the
> right to such measures of protection as are required by his status as a minor,
> on the part of his family, society and the State.
>
> **2.** Every child shall be registered immediately after birth and shall have
> a name.
>
> **3.** Every child has the right to acquire a nationality.

#### Human Rights Committee Interpretation

**General Comment No. 17 (1989)** interprets Article 24:

- The right to protection applies without discrimination, including
  discrimination based on the child's *birth status* (i.e., no distinction
  between children born in and out of marriage)
- Protection is owed by three sources: the family, society, and the state
- The measures of protection must be appropriate to the child's status as
  a minor

### Article 11 --- No Imprisonment for Debt

**Full text:**

> No one shall be imprisoned merely on the ground of inability to fulfil a
> contractual obligation.

This is one of the non-derogable provisions of the ICCPR --- states cannot
suspend it even in times of public emergency.

#### Key Interpretive Questions

**"Contractual obligation":** Article 11 is limited to obligations arising
from contract. The prevailing interpretation is that obligations arising from
statute (such as tax obligations or court-ordered child support) fall outside
Article 11's scope. This is a significant limitation.

**"Inability" vs. unwillingness:** The word "inability" indicates that the
person must be *incapable* of fulfilling the obligation, not merely unwilling.
The word "merely" indicates that if additional elements exist (such as fraud
or willful refusal), imprisonment is not prohibited.

**"Imprisonment":** The provision covers deprivation of liberty as a direct
consequence of inability to pay, including civil contempt incarceration.

#### Application to Child Support

The critical question is whether child support constitutes a "contractual
obligation" under Article 11:

- **Narrow view (majority position):** Child support obligations arise from
  statute and/or court order, not from contract. Article 11 therefore does
  not apply. This is the position most favorable to enforcement regimes
  that use incarceration.

- **Broader view:** The underlying norm --- that a person should not be
  imprisoned for *inability* (as distinct from refusal) to pay --- reflects
  a general principle of international law that extends beyond the technical
  "contractual" limitation. This view finds support in ECHR Protocol 4,
  which uses identical language, and in the general prohibition on debtors'
  prisons that exists in most developed legal systems.

- **Human Rights Committee:** The HRC has not issued a General Comment
  specifically addressing child support under Article 11. The absence of
  direct guidance leaves the question partly unresolved at the international
  level.

#### Practical Effect

Even under the narrow interpretation, Article 11 establishes a normative
principle: imprisonment should be a last resort for debt-like obligations,
and the distinction between inability and refusal matters. Many states that
have ratified the ICCPR have domestic laws that reflect this principle even
if they do not technically apply Article 11 to child support.

### Article 26 --- Non-Discrimination

**Full text:**

> All persons are equal before the law and are entitled without any
> discrimination to the equal protection of the law. In this respect, the law
> shall prohibit any discrimination and guarantee to all persons equal and
> effective protection against discrimination on any ground such as race,
> colour, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national or
> social origin, property, birth or other status.

#### Application to Custody and Support

Article 26 prohibits discrimination on the basis of sex in all areas of law,
including family law. The Human Rights Committee has held that the principle
of non-discrimination in Article 26 is not limited to rights provided for in
the Covenant itself --- it applies to *all* areas where a state provides
legal protection.

In *Zwaan-de Vries v. Netherlands* (Communication No. 182/1984), the HRC held
that denial of equal rights to married women as compared to married men
constitutes prohibited discrimination under Article 26.

**Application to family law:** Where a child support or custody system
systematically treats one gender differently from another --- whether in
presumptions about custody, assumptions about earning capacity, or enforcement
mechanisms --- Article 26 provides a basis for challenge. The protection
extends to discrimination based on "property" and "other status," which
could encompass economic status.

## 3. Hague Convention on Parental Responsibility and Protection of Children (1996)

**Full title:** Convention of 19 October 1996 on Jurisdiction, Applicable
Law, Recognition, Enforcement and Co-operation in Respect of Parental
Responsibility and Measures for the Protection of Children

**Concluded:** 19 October 1996
**Contracting parties:** 58 (as of September 2025)
**Scope:** Children from birth to age 18

### Convention Scope

The 1996 Convention is the broadest of the Hague family conventions, covering:

- **Custody and access** (parental responsibility)
- **Guardianship and curatorship**
- **Public care measures** (placement in foster care or institutional care)
- **Protection of children's property**
- **Representation of children**

### Key Mechanisms

1. **Jurisdiction:** The authorities of the state of the child's habitual
   residence have jurisdiction to take measures for the child's protection
   (Article 5). Jurisdiction shifts when habitual residence changes.

2. **Applicable law:** Authorities apply their own law when exercising
   jurisdiction (Article 15), but parental responsibility arising by
   operation of law is governed by the law of the child's habitual
   residence (Article 16).

3. **Recognition and enforcement:** Measures taken by authorities of one
   contracting state are recognized by operation of law in all other
   contracting states (Article 23). Enforcement requires a declaration of
   enforceability (Article 26).

4. **Cooperation:** Central Authorities in each contracting state facilitate
   communication and cooperation between jurisdictions.

### Conflicts Between Jurisdictions

When a child's habitual residence changes, jurisdiction shifts to the new
state of habitual residence. However, the 1996 Convention preserves
existing measures from the previous state until the new state modifies or
replaces them. This prevents a jurisdictional gap during transitions.

### Distinction from the Hague Child Abduction Convention (1980)

| Feature | 1980 Convention | 1996 Convention |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Wrongful removal/retention | Parental responsibility generally |
| **Mechanism** | Return of child to habitual residence | Recognition of custody orders |
| **Scope** | International abduction only | All aspects of child protection |
| **Parties** | 103 contracting states | 58 contracting states |
| **Remedies** | Prompt return or access | Recognition, enforcement, cooperation |

### Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (1980)

**Concluded:** 25 October 1980
**Entry into force:** 1 December 1983
**Contracting parties:** 103 (as of 2025)

#### Article 3 --- Wrongful Removal Defined

> The removal or the retention of a child is to be considered wrongful
> where ---
>
> (a) it is in breach of rights of custody attributed to a person, an
> institution or any other body, either jointly or alone, under the law of
> the State in which the child was habitually resident immediately before
> the removal or retention; and
>
> (b) at the time of removal or retention those rights were actually
> exercised, either jointly or alone, or would have been so exercised but
> for the removal or retention.

#### Article 12 --- Return Mechanism

If less than one year has elapsed between the date of wrongful removal or
retention and the commencement of proceedings, the court must order the
child's return forthwith, unless the respondent establishes one of the
limited exceptions. Even after one year, return is still the default unless
the child is "settled in its new environment."

#### Article 13 --- Exceptions (Grave Risk, Child's Objection)

A court is not bound to order return if the respondent establishes that:

> (a) the person, institution or other body having the care of the person
> of the child was not actually exercising the custody rights at the time
> of removal or retention, or had consented to or subsequently acquiesced
> in the removal or retention; or
>
> (b) there is a **grave risk** that his or her return would expose the
> child to physical or psychological harm or otherwise place the child in
> an **intolerable situation**.

The court may also refuse to order return if the child objects and has
attained an age and degree of maturity at which it is appropriate to take
account of the child's views.

The "grave risk" exception has been narrowly construed. In *X v. Latvia*,
the ECtHR held that the parent who opposes return must adduce "sufficient
evidence" of a specifically describable grave risk; unsubstantiated
allegations of abuse will not succeed.

#### Japan's Delayed Accession (2014) and Compliance Issues

Japan became a party to the 1980 Convention on 1 April 2014 --- the last
G7 nation to accede. Significant compliance issues persist:

- **No joint custody in Japanese law:** Japanese family law does not
  recognize joint custody, creating a structural conflict with the
  Convention's framework
- **Voluntary compliance only:** Compliance with Family Court rulings in
  Japan is essentially voluntary; there are no effective enforcement
  mechanisms for return orders
- **Broad "grave risk" interpretation:** Japanese courts have applied
  expansive interpretations of the Article 13(b) exception
- **No reported enforced returns:** There has not been one reported case
  in which a return order was actually enforced after a parent's refusal
  to comply
- **Cultural context:** Japan's approach to custody and visitation
  traditionally grants sole custody to one parent (typically the mother),
  with minimal non-custodial visitation rights

The U.S. State Department has placed and removed Japan from its list of
non-compliant countries multiple times. Advocates have characterized Japan
as a "black hole" for international child abduction cases.

## 4. UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)

**Adopted:** 20 November 1989
**Entry into force:** 2 September 1990
**State parties:** 196

The CRC is the most widely ratified human rights treaty in history.

### Key Articles

#### Article 3 --- Best Interest of the Child

> **1.** In all actions concerning children, whether undertaken by public or
> private social welfare institutions, courts of law, administrative
> authorities or legislative bodies, the best interests of the child shall
> be a primary consideration.
>
> **2.** States Parties undertake to ensure the child such protection and care
> as is necessary for his or her well-being, taking into account the rights
> and duties of his or her parents, legal guardians, or other individuals
> legally responsible for him or her, and, to this end, shall take all
> appropriate legislative and administrative measures.
>
> **3.** States Parties shall ensure that the institutions, services and
> facilities responsible for the care or protection of children shall conform
> with the standards established by competent authorities, particularly in
> the areas of safety, health, in the number and suitability of their staff,
> as well as competent supervision.

**Interpretation:** General Comment No. 14 (2013) of the Committee on the
Rights of the Child provides that the "best interests" assessment must be
individualized, must consider the child's own views, and must be conducted by
decision-makers with appropriate expertise. A formulaic or mechanical
application does not satisfy Article 3.

#### Article 7 --- Right to Know and Be Cared for by Parents

> **1.** The child shall be registered immediately after birth and shall have
> the right from birth to a name, the right to acquire a nationality and, as
> far as possible, the right to know and be cared for by his or her parents.

#### Article 9 --- Non-Separation from Parents

> **1.** States Parties shall ensure that a child shall not be separated from
> his or her parents against their will, except when competent authorities
> subject to judicial review determine, in accordance with applicable law and
> procedures, that such separation is necessary for the best interests of
> the child. Such determination may be necessary in a particular case such as
> one involving abuse or neglect of the child by the parents, or one where
> the parents are living separately and a decision must be made as to the
> child's place of residence.
>
> **2.** In any proceedings pursuant to paragraph 1 of the present article,
> all interested parties shall be given an opportunity to participate in the
> proceedings and make their views known.
>
> **3.** States Parties shall respect the right of the child who is separated
> from one or both parents to maintain personal relations and direct contact
> with both parents on a regular basis, except if it is contrary to the
> child's best interests.

**Dual principle:** Article 9 establishes (a) a presumption against
separation that can only be overcome by a best-interest finding following
due process, and (b) an affirmative right to maintain contact with *both*
parents following separation.

#### Article 12 --- Child's Right to Be Heard

> **1.** States Parties shall assure to the child who is capable of forming
> his or her own views the right to express those views freely in all matters
> affecting the child, the views of the child being given due weight in
> accordance with the age and maturity of the child.
>
> **2.** For this purpose, the child shall in particular be provided the
> opportunity to be heard in any judicial and administrative proceedings
> affecting the child, either directly, or through a representative or an
> appropriate body, in a manner consistent with the procedural rules of
> national law.

#### Article 18 --- Common Parental Responsibilities

> **1.** States Parties shall use their best efforts to ensure recognition of
> the principle that both parents have common responsibilities for the
> upbringing and development of the child. Parents or, as the case may be,
> legal guardians, have the primary responsibility for the upbringing and
> development of the child. The best interests of the child will be their
> basic concern.
>
> **2.** For the purpose of guaranteeing and promoting the rights set forth in
> the present Convention, States Parties shall render appropriate assistance
> to parents and legal guardians in the performance of their child-rearing
> responsibilities and shall ensure the development of institutions,
> facilities and services for the care of children.

**Key language:** "Both parents have *common* responsibilities" --- this
establishes a norm of shared parental responsibility that cuts against
systems built on sole-custody presumptions.

#### Article 27 --- Adequate Standard of Living

> **1.** States Parties recognize the right of every child to a standard of
> living adequate for the child's physical, mental, spiritual, moral and
> social development.
>
> **2.** The parent(s) or others responsible for the child have the primary
> responsibility to secure, within their abilities and financial capacities,
> the conditions of living necessary for the child's development.
>
> **3.** States Parties, in accordance with national conditions and within
> their means, shall take appropriate measures to assist parents and others
> responsible for the child to implement this right and shall in case of need
> provide material assistance and support programmes, particularly with
> regard to nutrition, clothing and housing.
>
> **4.** States Parties shall take all appropriate measures to secure the
> recovery of maintenance for the child from the parents or other persons
> having financial responsibility for the child, both within the State Party
> and from abroad.

**Note:** Article 27(2) qualifies the obligation: it is "within their
abilities and financial capacities." Article 27(4) creates a state
obligation to secure maintenance, but the underlying obligation on parents
is capacity-limited by Article 27(2). This tension --- between the state's
duty to collect and the parent's capacity limitation --- is precisely the
issue in enforcement regimes that do not perform ability-to-pay assessments.

### The US Exception

The United States is the **only UN member state** that has not ratified the
CRC.

**Timeline:**

- 1989: CRC adopted by the UN General Assembly
- 1995: President Clinton signed the CRC
- Present: The Senate has never voted on ratification

The US was one of three holdout states (alongside Somalia and South Sudan)
until those two countries ratified, leaving the US entirely alone.

**Reasons for Non-Ratification:**

1. **Sovereignty concerns:** Opponents argue the CRC encroaches on US
   sovereignty and would override domestic law, particularly state-level
   family law
2. **Parental rights ideology:** A coalition of conservative groups
   (including the Home School Legal Defense Association and the Heritage
   Foundation) has argued that the CRC would undermine parental authority,
   despite the CRC's repeated references to parental rights and
   responsibilities (Articles 5, 18)
3. **Federalism:** Family law in the US is primarily a state matter; the
   CRC would impose federal treaty obligations on state family courts
4. **Military recruitment:** The US military permits recruitment of
   17-year-olds, which conflicts with Optional Protocol provisions
5. **Political gridlock:** Senate Foreign Relations Committee opposition
   (notably under Chairman Jesse Helms) prevented the treaty from reaching
   a floor vote; subsequent administrations did not expend political capital
   on ratification

**Practical Implications of Non-Ratification:**

- US family courts are not bound by CRC standards
- The "best interest of the child" standard, while applied domestically, is
  not subject to international review or minimum standards
- No external body reviews US compliance with children's rights norms
- The US cannot be held to CRC Article 9 standards on non-separation
- The US cannot be held to CRC Article 27(2) standards on capacity-limited
  support obligations
- The absence of CRC ratification removes the normative constraint that
  would otherwise require proportionality review in child support
  enforcement

### CRC as Domestic Law

Some states have gone beyond ratification and directly incorporated the CRC
into domestic law, giving it the force of statute:

**Sweden:** Incorporated the CRC through the *United Nations Convention on
the Rights of the Child Act* (2018:1197), effective **1 January 2020**. This
gives the CRC the status of Swedish law, creating a direct obligation on
courts and legal practitioners to consider CRC rights in all decisions
affecting children.

**Norway:** Incorporated the CRC through the *Human Rights Act* of 21 May
1999, with CRC provisions effective **1 October 2003**. Under Norwegian law,
the CRC is directly applicable and *takes precedence* over conflicting
national statutes. This gives the CRC *supra-legislative* status --- it ranks
above ordinary law but below the Constitution.

**Effect of direct incorporation:** When the CRC is incorporated as domestic
law, individual litigants can invoke CRC articles directly in court
proceedings. A parent can argue that a custody determination violates
Article 9 or that a support order ignores Article 27(2)'s capacity
limitation. Without incorporation, the CRC operates only at the
inter-state level.

## 6. Structural Analysis: The Supranational Floor and the US Exception

The international frameworks establish a multi-layered supranational floor
for family law:

**Layer 1: Proportionality.** Under ECHR Article 8, any state interference
with family life must satisfy a proportionality test: it must be prescribed
by law, pursue a legitimate aim, and be "necessary in a democratic society."
This applies to custody determinations, contact restrictions, and
enforcement mechanisms. The ECtHR's case law (*Johansen*, *Gorgulu*,
*Strand Lobben*) establishes that permanent separation of parent and child
requires "particularly weighty reasons" and up-to-date evidence.

**Layer 2: No imprisonment for inability to pay.** Both ECHR Protocol 4
and ICCPR Article 11 prohibit imprisonment for inability to fulfill a
contractual obligation. While the technical scope is limited to "contractual"
obligations (excluding statutory child support in the prevailing
interpretation), the underlying norm --- that inability to pay should not
result in loss of liberty --- is reflected in the legal systems of virtually
all developed democracies.

**Layer 3: Non-separation and contact.** CRC Article 9 creates a
presumption against separation of children from parents, rebuttable only
by a best-interest finding following due process. CRC Article 9(3) creates
an affirmative right to contact with both parents. CRC Article 18 establishes
the norm of common (shared) parental responsibility.

**Layer 4: Capacity-limited obligations.** CRC Article 27(2) qualifies
the parental obligation to provide: it is "within their abilities and
financial capacities." This norm --- that a parent's obligation is limited
by actual capacity --- constrains enforcement regimes that impute income or
refuse to modify orders when circumstances change.

**The US position.** The United States is the only country in the world that
has both (a) not ratified the CRC and (b) maintains a child support
enforcement system (Title IV-D of the Social Security Act) that provides
federal financial incentives to states for enforcement. Under Title IV-D,
the federal government reimburses states $2 for every $3 spent on child
support enforcement, with additional incentive payments of up to 10% of
collections. This creates a structural incentive to maximize collections
regardless of ability to pay --- a perverse incentive that no supranational
norm constrains because the US has declined to ratify the one treaty (CRC)
that would impose a capacity limitation (Article 27(2)), and the one treaty
it has ratified (ICCPR) does not clearly extend its imprisonment-for-debt
prohibition (Article 11) to statutory obligations.

Every other developed democracy operates under at least one of these
supranational constraints. Most European states operate under all four
layers simultaneously (ECHR + ICCPR + CRC + Hague). The US stands alone
in having structured its family law enforcement system outside the reach
of international review.
