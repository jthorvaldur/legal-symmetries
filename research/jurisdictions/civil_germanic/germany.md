# Germany -- Death, Default, and Floor

## System Overview

Germany operates under the Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), a codified civil law system enacted in 1900 and continuously amended. The BGB governs inheritance, family law, obligations, and property. Above the BGB sits the Grundgesetz (GG) -- the Basic Law of the Federal Republic, enacted in 1949 by the Parliamentary Council (Parlamentarischer Rat). The Grundgesetz provides constitutional protection to marriage, family, and parental rights through Article 6. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG) enforces these constitutional guarantees and can strike down statutes that violate them.

Key structural features:

- **Codified defaults.** Every family configuration maps to a defined legal outcome. The system is algebraically closed.
- **Constitutional floor.** Parental rights, inheritance rights, and family integrity are constitutionally protected. Legislation cannot breach these floors without satisfying strict proportionality review.
- **Forced heirship (Pflichtteil).** Children and spouses receive a mandatory share of the estate that cannot be eliminated by testamentary disposition, except on extreme grounds (attempted murder, serious crime).
- **Shared parenting presumption.** Joint custody continues after separation. Transfer to sole custody requires a court finding that it serves the child's welfare.

## Biological Death Defaults

### Orders of Succession (Ordnungen)

German intestacy law (gesetzliche Erbfolge) organizes heirs into classes (Ordnungen). Any heir in a lower-numbered class excludes all heirs in higher-numbered classes entirely.

| Order | Who | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| First (Erste Ordnung) | Descendants of the deceased: children, grandchildren, great-grandchildren. Children inherit in equal shares. A predeceased child's share passes to that child's descendants by representation. | BGB SS 1924 |
| Second (Zweite Ordnung) | Parents of the deceased and their descendants (siblings, nieces, nephews). If both parents survive, they inherit equally. If one is predeceased, that parent's share passes to their descendants. | BGB SS 1925 |
| Third (Dritte Ordnung) | Grandparents and their descendants (uncles, aunts, cousins). | BGB SS 1926 |
| Fourth and higher | More remote ancestors and their descendants. Degree of relationship determined by number of intervening births (SS 1589). Relatives of equal degree inherit equal shares. | BGB SS 1928, 1929 |

### Spouse's Share Under Intestacy

| Alongside | Spouse's intestate share | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| First-order heirs (children) | 1/4 of the estate | BGB SS 1931(1) |
| Second-order heirs (parents, siblings) | 1/2 of the estate | BGB SS 1931(1) |
| Grandparents | 1/2 of the estate | BGB SS 1931(1) |
| No relatives of first, second, or third order | Entire estate | BGB SS 1931(2) |

### Zugewinngemeinschaft Adjustment

If the spouses were married under the default matrimonial property regime -- Zugewinngemeinschaft (community of accrued gains) -- and no marriage contract provides otherwise, the surviving spouse's intestate share is increased by an additional 1/4 as a lump-sum equalization of gains (pauschaler Zugewinnausgleich). This increase applies regardless of whether any actual gain was accrued during the marriage.

| Scenario | Spouse's total share | Calculation | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zugewinngemeinschaft + first-order heirs (children) | **1/2** of the estate | 1/4 (SS 1931) + 1/4 (SS 1371) | BGB SS 1931(3), SS 1371(1) |
| Zugewinngemeinschaft + second-order heirs | **3/4** of the estate | 1/2 (SS 1931) + 1/4 (SS 1371) | BGB SS 1931(3), SS 1371(1) |

**Example:** Decedent leaves an estate of EUR 100,000, a surviving spouse, and two children. Under Zugewinngemeinschaft: the spouse receives 1/2 (EUR 50,000). The two children split the remaining 1/2 equally (EUR 25,000 each).

**Alternative option:** The surviving spouse may reject the inheritance and instead claim the specifically calculated Zugewinnausgleich (equalization of actual accrued gains) plus the Pflichtteil. This may be more favorable when the spouse's actual gain is large relative to the estate. (BGB SS 1371(2), (3))

## The Pflichtteil -- The Unbreachable Floor

The Pflichtteil (compulsory share / forced heirship) is a monetary claim against the heir(s), not a share of the estate itself. It arises automatically by operation of law when a person entitled to it is excluded from inheritance by will. The testator **cannot** eliminate it by testamentary disposition. It is the strongest forced heirship mechanism in any Western legal system.

### Who Gets It

| Entitled Person | Pflichtteil Amount | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Descendants (children, grandchildren) | 1/2 of what they would receive under intestacy | BGB SS 2303(1) |
| Surviving spouse | 1/2 of what they would receive under intestacy | BGB SS 2303(2) |
| Parents (only if decedent has no descendants) | 1/2 of what they would receive under intestacy | BGB SS 2303(2) |

**Not entitled:** Siblings, grandparents, more remote relatives. Only the innermost family circle is protected.

### Practical Calculations

| Family Configuration (Zugewinngemeinschaft) | Spouse's intestate share | Spouse's Pflichtteil | Children's intestate share (each, 2 children) | Child's Pflichtteil (each) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Spouse + 2 children | 1/2 | **1/4** | 1/4 | **1/8** |
| Spouse + 1 child | 1/2 | **1/4** | 1/2 | **1/4** |
| Spouse + 3 children | 1/2 | **1/4** | 1/6 | **1/12** |

### Nature of the Right

- It is a **monetary claim** (Geldanspruch), not a right to specific assets.
- The Pflichtteilsberechtigter (compulsory heir) is not an heir -- they are a creditor of the estate.
- The claim arises at the moment of death and is immediately enforceable against the heir(s).
- The heir must provide a complete inventory of the estate and, if requested, a valuation at the estate's expense (BGB SS 2314).
- Gifts made by the decedent within 10 years before death are added back to the estate for Pflichtteil calculation (Pflichtteilserganzungsanspruch, BGB SS 2325).

### Grounds for Denial (Pflichtteilsentziehung)

The compulsory share can be denied **only** on the grounds enumerated in BGB SS 2333. These grounds are exhaustive and narrowly construed:

| Ground | BGB SS 2333(1) Nr. |
|---|---|
| Attempts on the life of the testator, testator's spouse, another descendant, or a similarly close person (nach dem Leben trachten) | Nr. 1 |
| Commits a crime (Verbrechen) or serious intentional offense (schweres vorsatzliches Vergehen) against one of the protected persons | Nr. 2 |
| Maliciously violates the statutory duty to maintain the testator (boswillige Verletzung der Unterhaltspflicht) | Nr. 3 |
| Convicted by final judgment of an intentional crime and sentenced to imprisonment of at least one year without probation, and continued participation in the estate is unreasonable for the testator | Nr. 4 |

**Critically:** Mere estrangement, disagreement, lifestyle disapproval, or even prolonged absence is **not** a ground for denial. The threshold is extreme misconduct approaching or constituting violence, crime, or deliberate harm.

### Constitutional Status

The Bundesverfassungsgericht confirmed in BVerfGE 112, 332 (April 19, 2005, 1 BvR 1644/00) that the Pflichtteil has constitutional protection under Article 14(1) GG (property and inheritance guarantee) in conjunction with Article 6(1) GG (protection of family). The court held:

- Children's right to a minimum economic participation in the estate is a constitutionally guaranteed fundamental right.
- The Pflichtteil represents a "bedarfsunabhangige Mindestbeteiligung" -- a needs-independent minimum participation grounded in family solidarity.
- The existing grounds for denial in SS 2333 are compatible with the Basic Law.

**Comparison to US law:** In most US states, a testator can completely disinherit their children with no grounds required. The surviving spouse typically has an elective share (usually 1/3), but children have no forced heirship protection at all. Louisiana is the only US state with a forced heirship system, and it is limited to children under 24 or disabled children.

## Parental Death Defaults

### Sorgerecht (Parental Care / Custody)

| Event | Default Rule | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Child born to married parents | Both parents have joint parental care (gemeinsames Sorgerecht) automatically | BGB SS 1626(1) |
| Parents separate or divorce | **Joint custody continues.** Separation does not alter parental care. | BGB SS 1626(1), (3) |
| One parent seeks sole custody | Court may transfer sole custody **only** if it serves the child's best interest (Kindeswohl) | BGB SS 1671(1) |
| Both parents consent to sole custody | Court transfers to requesting parent if not contrary to child's welfare | BGB SS 1671(1) Nr. 1 |
| One parent applies without other's consent | Court transfers sole custody only if transfer is expected to best serve child's welfare | BGB SS 1671(1) Nr. 2 |
| Child born to unmarried parents | Mother has sole custody initially | BGB SS 1626a(3) |
| Unmarried parents make Sorgeerklarung (custody declaration) | Joint custody established; declarations must be notarized, can be made before birth | BGB SS 1626a(1) Nr. 1 |
| Unmarried parents marry | Joint custody arises automatically | BGB SS 1626a(1) Nr. 2 |
| Unmarried father seeks joint custody, mother refuses | Father can apply to family court; court grants joint custody if it serves the child's welfare | BGB SS 1626a(2) |

### Key Principles

1. **Joint custody is the default and the norm.** It continues after separation. No parent "wins" or "loses" custody by default.
2. **Sole custody is the exception.** It requires an affirmative judicial finding that sole custody serves the child's welfare -- not merely that it is convenient, or that one parent's attorney drafted a judgment.
3. **The child's welfare (Kindeswohl) is the sole criterion.** Not parental convenience, not income disparity, not gender.
4. **Both parents retain decision-making authority** over matters of significant importance (Angelegenheiten von erheblicher Bedeutung) under joint custody. Day-to-day matters (Angelegenheiten des taglichen Lebens) are decided by the parent with whom the child currently resides. (BGB SS 1687)

### Constitutional Protection

Article 6(2) GG states: "Pflege und Erziehung der Kinder sind das naturliche Recht der Eltern und die zuvorderst ihnen obliegende Pflicht" -- "Care and upbringing of children are the natural right of parents and the duty primarily incumbent upon them."

The Bundesverfassungsgericht enforces this with strict proportionality review:

- **BVerfG, Order of 14 September 2021 (1 BvR 1525/20):** State interventions in parental rights may only occur when the child's welfare is endangered. The principle of proportionality requires that less intrusive measures be exhausted before custody is altered.
- **BVerfG, Order of 21 July 2010 (1 BvR 420/09):** Exclusion of the father from parental custody when the mother refuses consent is unconstitutional -- it violates Art. 6(2) GG. This forced the legislature to reform SS 1626a to give fathers a path to joint custody through court application.
- **BVerfG, 2024 (1 BvR 2017/21):** Reinforced rights of biological fathers, holding that biological fathers are holders of the right of parental care under Art. 6(2) GG and can invoke that right even against the legal father.

The constitutional standard: **parental rights are a pre-state, natural right.** The state is the guardian of the child's welfare (Wachteramt), not a substitute parent. State intervention is justified only as a last resort when the child is endangered, not when the state disagrees with parental decisions.

### 1998 Kindschaftsrechtsreform

The Child Law Reform Act (Kindschaftsrechtsreformgesetz) of December 16, 1997, effective July 1, 1998, made the following structural changes:

- Eliminated the automatic grant of sole custody to the mother upon divorce
- Established joint custody as the continuing default after separation
- Created the Sorgeerklarung mechanism for unmarried parents
- Equalized the legal status of children born in and out of wedlock

## Kindesunterhalt -- Child Support

### The Dusseldorfer Tabelle

Child support in Germany is calculated using the Dusseldorfer Tabelle (Dusseldorf Table), published by the Oberlandesgericht Dusseldorf in coordination with other higher regional courts. It is not a statute but a guideline applied uniformly by family courts across all 16 federal states (Bundeslander).

### 2026 Minimum Monthly Amounts (Income Group 1: net income up to EUR 2,100)

| Child's Age | Monthly Needs (EUR) | After Child Benefit Offset (EUR) |
|---|---|---|
| 0-5 years | 486 | 361 (half of EUR 250 child benefit deducted) |
| 6-11 years | 558 | 433 |
| 12-17 years | 653 | 528 |
| 18+ (adult child) | 698 | 448 (full EUR 250 child benefit deducted) |

### Structure

- **15 income groups** from EUR 0 - 2,100 (Group 1) up to EUR 11,200+ (Group 15)
- Amounts increase with income group and child's age
- **Selbstbehalt (self-retention):** The obligor retains a minimum for their own subsistence: EUR 1,450/month (employed) or EUR 1,200/month (unemployed) for minor children and privileged adult children

### Consequences of Non-Payment

| Mechanism | Legal Basis | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Civil enforcement | ZPO SS 704 et seq. | Wage garnishment, asset seizure |
| Criminal prosecution | StGB SS 170 (Verletzung der Unterhaltspflicht) | Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine |
| Aggravated case (pregnant woman, causing abortion) | StGB SS 170(2) | Imprisonment up to 5 years or fine |
| State advancement (Unterhaltsvorschuss) | Unterhaltsvorschussgesetz (UVG) | State pays the child and pursues the obligor |

**StGB SS 170 in detail:** A parent who evades their statutory maintenance obligation such that the necessities of the entitled person are endangered -- or would be endangered without the assistance of others -- commits a criminal offense. The prosecution requires proof of both the obligation and the **ability** to pay. Inability to pay is a defense; willful evasion is a crime.

This is not a debtors' prison. It is criminal punishment for the willful act of evading a known obligation while having the means to fulfill it. The distinction is constitutionally significant.

## Financial Death Defaults -- Insolvency

### Insolvenzordnung (InsO) Framework

| Event | Default | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Insolvency filed (natural person) | Insolvency proceedings opened; trustee appointed; assets distributed to creditors | InsO SS 1, SS 27 |
| Discharge application | Debtor applies for Restschuldbefreiung (discharge of residual debt) | InsO SS 286 |
| Good conduct period (Wohlverhaltensperiode) | **3 years** (reduced from 6 years by reform effective 2021). Debtor assigns attachable income to trustee. | InsO SS 287(2) |
| Conditions during good conduct period | Debtor must be gainfully employed or seek employment; must comply with disclosure obligations; must not incur inappropriate new debts | InsO SS 295 |
| Discharge granted | All ordinary debts are extinguished; creditors permanently barred from enforcement | InsO SS 301 |

### Claims That Survive Discharge (InsO SS 302)

| Excluded Claim Type | Survives Discharge? | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Maintenance/child support obligations intentionally unpaid | **Yes -- survives** | InsO SS 302 Nr. 1 |
| Debts from intentional unlawful acts (vorsatzlich begangene unerlaubte Handlung) | **Yes -- survives** | InsO SS 302 Nr. 1 |
| Tax debts from criminal tax evasion | **Yes -- survives** | InsO SS 302 Nr. 1 |
| Criminal fines and penalty payments | **Yes -- survives** | InsO SS 302 Nr. 2 |
| Interest-free loans to cover insolvency costs | **Yes -- survives** | InsO SS 302 Nr. 3 |
| Ordinary commercial debts | No -- discharged | InsO SS 301 |

**The principle:** Discharge benefits only honest debtors (redliche Schuldner). Those who willfully evade maintenance, commit intentional torts, or engage in tax fraud cannot use insolvency to escape these obligations. Child support is explicitly protected.

## Forced Heirship / Floor Mechanisms -- Summary

| Floor | Mechanism | Can It Be Breached? | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pflichtteil** | 50% of intestate share as monetary claim, arises automatically upon death | Only for BGB SS 2333 grounds (attempted murder, serious crime against testator/family, malicious failure to maintain, imprisonment of 1+ year) | BGB SS 2303-2338; BVerfGE 112, 332 |
| **Parental rights** | Joint custody is the default, continues after separation | Only by court finding that sole custody serves child's welfare (Kindeswohl) | BGB SS 1626, SS 1671; GG Art. 6(2) |
| **Constitutional family protection** | Marriage and family under special state protection; parental care is a natural right | Federal Constitutional Court enforces via proportionality review; state intervention only as last resort | GG Art. 6(1), (2) |
| **Child support survives insolvency** | Maintenance obligations excluded from discharge | Cannot be discharged through bankruptcy when willfully unpaid | InsO SS 302 |
| **Criminal sanction for support evasion** | Imprisonment up to 3 years for willful evasion | Requires proof of ability to pay; inability is a defense | StGB SS 170 |
| **No debtors' prison for inability** | Imprisonment only for willful evasion, not for inability to pay | Protected by GG Art. 2(2) (freedom of person is inviolable), Art. 104 (deprivation of liberty requires judicial order and statutory basis), ECHR Protocol 4 Art. 1 (ratified 1968), ICCPR Art. 11 | GG Art. 2(2), Art. 104; ECHR Protocol 4 Art. 1; ICCPR Art. 11 |

## Who Wrote the Defaults?

| Default | Author | Date | Whose Interest |
|---|---|---|---|
| Intestacy / Pflichtteil | Imperial legislature (Reichstag), maintained by Bundestag | 1900 (BGB enacted), Pflichtteil constitutional status confirmed 2005 | Children and spouse equally -- ensures minimum economic participation regardless of testator's will |
| Zugewinngemeinschaft | Bundestag | 1958 (Gleichberechtigungsgesetz -- Equal Rights Act replaced husband's administration of wife's property) | Surviving spouse -- lump-sum gain equalization prevents manipulation of marital property at death |
| Shared parenting default | Bundestag | 1998 (Kindschaftsrechtsreformgesetz) | Children -- both parents remain responsible after separation |
| Unmarried father's path to custody | Bundestag, forced by BVerfG | 2013 reform of SS 1626a (following BVerfG 2010 ruling) | Fathers and children -- mother's veto over joint custody held unconstitutional |
| Grundgesetz Art. 6 | Parlamentarischer Rat (Parliamentary Council) | 1949 | Family as institution; parental rights as pre-state natural right |
| Dusseldorfer Tabelle | OLG Dusseldorf + coordinating OLGs | First published 1962, updated annually | Children -- standardized, predictable support amounts based on obligor's income |
| Insolvency discharge | Bundestag | InsO enacted 1994, discharge period reduced to 3 years in 2021 | Honest debtors -- fresh start; but maintenance creditors and tort victims protected |

## Anonymized Parallel

In German law, a parent's rights cannot be severed by an allocation judgment signed without meaningful review. Joint custody is the constitutional default and continues after separation. To change this requires an affirmative court finding about the child's welfare -- not merely one parent's counsel drafting a judgment for the other to sign. The Bundesverfassungsgericht has repeatedly held that a parent's exclusion from custody without judicial review violates Article 6(2) of the Grundgesetz.

The Pflichtteil principle extends beyond inheritance. It embodies a structural axiom: the system has a floor below which rights cannot fall. A child cannot be disinherited without extreme cause. A parent cannot be separated from their child without a welfare finding. A support obligation cannot be discharged through insolvency. These are not discretionary -- they are mandatory defaults written by the legislature and enforced by the constitutional court.

The German system distinguishes between inability and refusal. A parent who cannot pay child support is not imprisoned -- inability is a defense under StGB SS 170. A parent who willfully evades payment commits a criminal offense. This distinction is constitutionally required: Article 2(2) GG protects the freedom of the person; ECHR Protocol 4, Article 1 prohibits imprisonment for contractual inability. The system punishes the willful act, not the condition.

Where a legal system permits the severance of parental rights without a welfare finding, permits disinheritance of children without cause, and imprisons for inability rather than refusal -- that system has no floor. The German system was designed to have one. Every default was written by a legislature, reviewed by a constitutional court, and grounded in the principle that family solidarity creates obligations that survive death, divorce, and insolvency.
