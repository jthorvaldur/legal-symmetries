# Sweden -- Death, Default, and Floor

## System Overview

Sweden operates under the **Nordic civil law tradition**, with codified statutes forming the backbone of private law. Two codes dominate the topics covered here: the **Arvdabalken** (AB, Code of Inheritance, 1958:637) for succession, and the **Foraldrabalken** (FB, Children and Parents Code, 1949:381) for parental rights, custody, residence, and contact. Financial support is governed partly by the FB and partly through the **Forsakringskassan** (Social Insurance Agency), which administers a state-backed child maintenance system with no direct parallel in US law. Personal insolvency falls under the **Skuldsaneringslagen** (Debt Restructuring Act, 2016:675).

Sweden is a signatory to the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR, ratified 1952), making ECHR Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life) enforceable through the European Court of Human Rights in Strasbourg. In 2020, Sweden incorporated the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (Barnkonventionen) directly into domestic law through the **Lag (2018:1197) om FN:s konvention om barnets rattigheter**, effective January 1, 2020.

The Swedish system is built on a principle that is the structural opposite of American family law's adversarial allocation model: **joint custody is the default that survives separation, and the system is designed to make the elimination of one parent from a child's life structurally difficult.** Sweden was the first country in the world to legislate joint custody for divorced parents (1977) and later for unmarried parents (1998). Roughly 30-40% of children with separated parents live in alternating residence (vaxelvist boende) -- the highest rate in the world. The infrastructure exists to make this work: 480 days of parental leave with 90 days reserved for each parent, universal daycare, and a state-administered child support backstop that pays the custodial parent directly when the obligor defaults.

## Biological Death Defaults (Arvsratt -- Intestacy)

When a person dies without a will, the Arvdabalken provides automatic distribution rules through three classes of heirs (arvsklasser), following the parentel system. The first class inherits before the second; the second before the third. Within each class, the iure representationis principle applies: if a direct heir has predeceased the decedent, that heir's children step into their place.

### Inheritance Classes

| Class | Who | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| First class (forsta arvsklassen) | Children (brostarvingar), grandchildren by representation | AB 2:1 |
| Second class (andra arvsklassen) | Parents; if a parent is dead, siblings and their descendants | AB 2:2 |
| Third class (tredje arvsklassen) | Grandparents; if dead, uncles/aunts (but NOT their children -- no further representation) | AB 2:3-2:4 |

### Spouse's Rights

| Heirs | Share | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Spouse + common children only | Spouse inherits **everything** with free disposition right (fri forfoganderatt); children receive **efterarv** (after-inheritance) when the surviving spouse dies | AB 3:1 |
| Spouse + non-common children (sarkullbarn) | Sarkullbarn can claim their share **immediately**; spouse inherits the remainder with fri forfoganderatt | AB 3:1 |
| Spouse only (no children, no second-class heirs) | Spouse inherits **everything** with full ownership (aganderatt) | AB 3:1 |
| No spouse, children only | Children split equally per capita, with representation per stirpes | AB 2:1 |

### The Basbeloppsregeln (Basic Amount Rule)

The surviving spouse has a guaranteed **minimum inheritance** of property worth at least **four prisbasbelopp** (four price base amounts, approximately SEK 228,000 in 2025). This guarantee takes precedence over testamentary dispositions and even over sarkullbarn's immediate inheritance rights. If a sarkullbarn's share must be reduced to satisfy the basbeloppsregeln, that child receives a right to efterarv instead -- they will inherit when the surviving spouse dies.

| Protection | Amount | Trumps | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Basbeloppsregeln | 4 prisbasbelopp (~SEK 228,000) | Will provisions AND sarkullbarn's immediate share | AB 3:1 para. 2 |

**Key distinction from common law:** In Sweden, the surviving spouse receives property with **fri forfoganderatt** (free disposition right), not full ownership. The spouse may use and spend the property during their lifetime, but cannot give it away by will to someone other than the children's detriment. When the surviving spouse dies, the children's share (efterarv) is calculated as a proportion of the second estate.

## Laglott -- The Compulsory Share

The laglott is the Swedish forced heirship mechanism. It cannot be overridden by will except in extremely narrow circumstances. It protects only **brostarvingar** (direct descendants: children, grandchildren, etc.) -- the surviving spouse has no laglott.

### Compulsory Share Table

| Who | Amount | Can It Be Denied? | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Children (brostarvingar) | **50%** of their intestate share (arvslott) | Very rarely -- only for serious criminal conduct against the testator | AB 7:1 |
| Example: 2 children, no spouse | Each child's arvslott = 50% of estate; laglott = 25% of estate each | Same | AB 7:1 |
| Example: 3 children, no spouse | Each child's arvslott = 33.3%; laglott = 16.67% each | Same | AB 7:1 |

### How Laglott Works

A testator can write a will leaving everything to someone other than their children. But the children have the right to **jamka** (challenge/adjust) the will by requesting their laglott. This is not automatic -- the child must actively claim it within six months of being notified of the will (AB 7:3). If the child does not claim, the will stands as written.

### Enhanced Laglott Protection (Forstarkt laglottsskydd)

AB 7:4 provides protection against lifetime gifts designed to circumvent the laglott. If the deceased made gifts during their lifetime that are functionally equivalent to testamentary dispositions (gava som ar att likstalla med testamente), the children can bring an action to have those gifts reduced to restore their laglott. This prevents the common avoidance strategy of giving assets away before death.

### Grounds for Forfeiture (Forverkande)

Forfeiture of inheritance rights is governed by **AB 15:1-15:3** (not Chapter 7 as sometimes cited):

| Ground | Effect | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Heir intentionally killed the decedent through criminal conduct | Complete forfeiture of inheritance and testamentary rights | AB 15:1 |
| Exception: perpetrator under 15, or suffering from serious mental illness | Forfeiture may not apply | AB 15:1 |
| Heir destroyed or concealed a will through coercion or abuse of position | Forfeiture of rights under the will | AB 15:2 |
| Heir through coercion caused testator to make or revoke a will | Forfeiture | AB 15:2 |

**Critical note:** There is no general "disinheritance for bad behavior" mechanism in Swedish law comparable to what exists in some civil law systems. A parent cannot disinherit a child simply for estrangement, ingratitude, or lifestyle choices. The only grounds for forfeiture are serious criminal conduct (essentially murder) or will tampering. The laglott is effectively irrevocable.

## Parental Authority Defaults -- The Swedish Model

This is the most important section of this document, because Sweden has constructed the world's strongest structural protection against what might be called "parental death by allocation judgment."

### Historical Arc: From Maternal Presumption to Shared Parenting Default

| Year | Reform | What Changed | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-1977 | Maternal presumption | Mother received custody upon divorce as the default | Former FB provisions |
| **1977** | First joint custody reform | Joint custody (gemensam vardnad) became **available** for divorced parents for the first time -- parents could agree to it | FB reform, Prop. 1975/76:170 |
| **1983** | Extension | Joint custody could be ordered by agreement even for unmarried parents | FB reform |
| **1998** | Landmark reform | Joint custody became the **default** for all parents. Courts gained power to order joint custody **against one parent's wishes** if in the child's best interest. Unmarried parents could obtain joint custody by simple registration at Skatteverket (tax authority). | FB 6:4, 6:5; Prop. 1997/98:7 |
| **2006** | Recalibration | Court practice under the 1998 reform was criticized for imposing joint custody in cases involving domestic violence. The 2006 reform clarified that ordering joint custody against a parent's wishes should be done with **great care** (stor forsiktighet). No presumption either for or against joint custody. Risk assessment for violence/abuse made mandatory. | FB 6:2a, 6:5; Prop. 2005/06:99 |
| **2021** | Strengthened child rights perspective | New mandatory information meetings before court proceedings. Clearer duty to hear the child. Focus shifted from parents' ability to cooperate to parents' ability to **prioritize the child's needs**. Umgangessabotage (contact sabotage) recognized as grounds for custody transfer. | FB 6:2a (amended); Prop. 2020/21:150 |
| **2025** | Latest amendment | FB 6:2a reformulated: risk of harm to the child is given more prominent place. The child's need for contact with both parents is no longer explicitly listed as a separate special circumstance -- absorbed into the general best interest assessment. | FB 6:2a (amended Jan 1, 2025) |

### Current Default Rules

| Event | Default | Override Requires | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Child born to married parents | Joint custody automatic | N/A | FB 6:3 |
| Child born to unmarried parents | Mother has sole custody; joint custody by registration with Skatteverket or social welfare committee | Joint notification by both parents | FB 6:3, 6:4 |
| Parents separate or divorce | **Joint custody CONTINUES as default** | Court finding that joint custody is contrary to child's best interest | FB 6:5 |
| One parent seeks sole custody | Court must find joint custody contrary to child's best interest | Judicial determination based on barnets basta (best interest of the child) | FB 6:5 |
| Residence after separation | Court or agreement determines; vaxelvist boende (alternating residence) is common and explicitly provided for | Best interest analysis under FB 6:14a | FB 6:14a |
| Contact with non-residential parent | Child has a **right** to contact (umgangesratt) with the parent they do not live with | Court may limit contact only if necessary for child's protection | FB 6:15 |
| Contact sabotage (umgangessabotage) | Established sabotage may lead to transfer of custody to the other parent | Judicial determination | FB 6:5 (case law), Prop. 2020/21:150 |

### FB 6:2a -- The Best Interest Principle (Barnets Basta)

The child's best interest shall be **decisive** (avgorande) for all decisions regarding custody, residence, and contact. When assessing what is best for the child, special attention shall be paid to:

1. The risk that the child or anyone in the family is subjected to **violence or other abuse** (vald eller annan krankning)
2. The risk that the child is unlawfully taken or retained
3. The child's need for a close and good relationship with **both parents** (note: the 2025 amendment restructured but did not eliminate this principle)

### Vaxelvist Boende -- Alternating Residence

Sweden has the highest rate of alternating residence (children splitting time roughly equally between separated parents' homes) in the world.

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Children with separated parents in alternating residence | **~35%** | SCB (Statistics Sweden); Bergstrom et al. |
| Historical comparison (1980s) | ~1% | SCB |
| Total children in alternating residence | ~200,000 | SCB estimates |

**Research outcomes (Malin Bergstrom, Karolinska Institutet / Stockholm University):**

- Children in joint physical custody report **better well-being and mental health** than children living mostly or only with one parent (Bergstrom et al., BMC Public Health 2013)
- Preschool children in joint physical custody show **fewer psychological symptoms** than those living with one parent (Bergstrom et al., Acta Paediatrica 2018)
- No Swedish studies have found children's health to be **worse** in joint physical custody than in sole parental care from age 3 and beyond
- Shared residence enables children to maintain relationships with both parents regarding both **emotional support and relationship quality**, on par with children in intact families

### Infrastructure That Makes It Work

Sweden did not simply legislate shared parenting -- it built the infrastructure to support it:

| Infrastructure | How It Supports Shared Parenting |
|---|---|
| **480 days parental leave** (foraldraforsakring) | 240 days per parent; 90 days reserved for each parent and **non-transferable**. Forces both parents to take substantial leave. Introduced 1974; "daddy month" added 1995; second reserved month 2002; third reserved month 2016. |
| **Universal daycare** (forskola) | All children aged 1-5 have a right to a place in forskola, regardless of parents' employment status. Municipality must provide within 4 months. Fees are income-based and capped (maxtaxa). |
| **Free schooling** (grundskola) | 9-year compulsory education, free of charge, including school meals. |
| **Forsakringskassan backstop** | If the non-resident parent does not pay child support, the state pays underhallsstod directly to the custodial parent and recovers from the obligor. The child is never without support. |
| **Samarbetssamtal** (cooperation talks) | Municipal family law units (familjeratt) offer free mediation/cooperation talks to separated parents. Mandatory information meetings before court proceedings (since 2022). |

### The Structural Contrast with US Allocation Judgments

In Sweden, the concept of "parental death" via allocation judgment is **structurally impossible**. The system operates as follows:

1. Joint custody is the **default** that survives separation (FB 6:5)
2. To remove joint custody, a court must find it **contrary to the child's best interest** -- the burden is on the party seeking sole custody, not on the parent seeking to retain rights
3. Even when sole custody is awarded, the non-custodial parent retains **contact rights** (umgangesratt) (FB 6:15)
4. Contact can only be restricted when necessary for the **child's protection** -- not because one parent's attorney drafted a judgment
5. A parent who sabotages the other parent's contact risks **losing custody** (umgangessabotage doctrine)
6. Enforcement of contact orders uses **vitesforelaggande** (penalty fines) -- the court can impose recurring fines for each missed contact occasion (FB Chapter 21)

In many US jurisdictions, one parent can be "parentally killed" by an allocation judgment. In Sweden, the system is designed to prevent exactly this. The default is that both parents remain parents. The system requires an affirmative judicial finding, based on the child's best interest, to deviate from that default. And even then, the deviation is limited -- contact rights survive.

## Financial Death Defaults (Underhallsbidrag and Underhallsstod)

### The Dual System

Sweden operates a unique dual system for child support:

1. **Underhallsbidrag** (maintenance allowance): The private obligation between parents, determined by agreement or court order. Both parents contribute proportionally to the child's needs based on their respective financial capacity.
2. **Underhallsstod** (maintenance support): A state-funded backstop paid by Forsakringskassan directly to the custodial parent when the obligor does not pay.

### Private Support (Underhallsbidrag)

| Feature | Detail | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Duration | Until child turns 18, or 21 if in full-time education | FB 7:1 |
| Calculation | Based on child's needs and both parents' financial capacity; no statutory formula | FB 7:1 |
| Form | Agreement (oral or written) or court order | FB 7:2 |
| Modification | Either parent can seek modification based on changed circumstances | FB 7:8 |

### State Backstop (Underhallsstod)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| When paid | When non-resident parent does not pay, pays less than SEK 1,273/month, or paternity is not established |
| Amounts (2025) | SEK 1,673/month (child under 7); SEK 1,823/month (7-14); SEK 2,223/month (15+) |
| Who pays | Forsakringskassan pays the custodial parent; then calculates how much the obligor parent must repay to the state |
| Recovery | Forsakringskassan recovers from the obligor based on their income -- the obligor owes the state, not the other parent |
| Enforcement | Kronofogden (Swedish Enforcement Authority) handles recovery through utmatning (wage garnishment), property seizure |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | How It Works | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Utmatning (wage garnishment)** | Kronofogden deducts directly from debtor's salary, leaving a protected minimum (forbehallsbelopp) | Kronofogden (Enforcement Authority) |
| **Property seizure** | Bank accounts, personal property, and real property can be seized and sold | Kronofogden |
| **Debt registration** | Non-payers are registered in Kronofogden's records, affecting creditworthiness | Kronofogden |
| **Forsakringskassan subrogation** | When the state pays underhallsstod, it acquires the right to recover from the obligor; the obligor now owes the state, not the other parent | Forsakringskassan |

### Imprisonment for Non-Payment: The Critical Difference

**Sweden does not imprison parents for non-payment of child support.** Sweden abolished imprisonment for debt (galdstugan) in **1879**. There is no civil contempt mechanism for child support enforcement and no criminal offense of non-payment comparable to the French *abandon de famille* or the US contempt power.

The enforcement model is purely **administrative and civil**: wage garnishment, property seizure, and debt registration. The state (Forsakringskassan) absorbs the payment risk by paying the custodial parent directly and pursuing recovery from the obligor through Kronofogden. If the obligor genuinely cannot pay, the child is still supported -- the state bears the cost.

This is the structural opposite of the US model, where non-payment can lead to incarceration through civil contempt proceedings, creating the coercive paradox identified in *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431 (2011): the purge condition (pay the support) becomes impossible to satisfy from inside a jail cell.

| Feature | Sweden | United States (typical) |
|---|---|---|
| Non-payment enforcement | Administrative: wage garnishment, property seizure | Civil contempt: incarceration until purge |
| State backstop | Forsakringskassan pays child directly | No federal backstop; some state TANF assistance |
| Criminal prosecution | Not available for support non-payment | Federal CSRA (18 USC 228) for willful interstate non-payment |
| Imprisonment possible? | **No** | **Yes** -- both civil contempt and criminal prosecution |
| Who bears the risk of non-payment? | The state (taxpayers) | The child and custodial parent |

## Insolvency and Child Support (Skuldsaneringslagen)

The Skuldsaneringslagen (2016:675) provides debt restructuring for individuals who are unable to pay their debts and whose financial situation is unlikely to improve in the foreseeable future. The standard repayment plan runs for five years, after which remaining eligible debts are discharged.

### Does Child Support Survive?

| Question | Answer | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Can underhallsbidrag (private support) be discharged? | **No.** Child support obligations are exempt from debt restructuring. | Skuldsaneringslagen (2016:675) |
| Exception | If Forsakringskassan or a foreign public authority has assumed the right to receive the child support (i.e., the state has subrogated), the debt to the state agency may be included in restructuring | Skuldsaneringslagen (2016:675) |
| Can enforcement continue during restructuring? | **Yes** for alimentary debts not covered by the restructuring order | Skuldsaneringslagen |
| Statute of limitations on arrears | Five years from due date | Preskriptionslagen (1981:130) |

**Summary:** Child support obligations owed directly to a child or custodial parent survive debt restructuring in Sweden. The only scenario where a support-related debt might be included is when Forsakringskassan has paid underhallsstod and subrogated into the obligor's debt -- in that case, the state's recovery claim (not the child's right to support) may potentially be restructured. The child's right to ongoing support is never compromised.

## Constitutional and Supranational Protection

### Regeringsformen (Instrument of Government, 1974:152)

Chapter 2 of the Regeringsformen (RF) sets out fundamental rights and freedoms. While Sweden's constitutional tradition is less rights-adjudicative than the US model (no tradition of judicial review comparable to *Marbury v. Madison*), the 2010 constitutional reform strengthened the role of courts in reviewing legislation for constitutional compliance.

| Provision | Protection | Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| RF 1:2 | Public institutions shall protect the private and family lives of individuals | General duty on the state |
| RF 2:6 | Protection against bodily punishment and other physical violations | Relevant to custody decisions involving violence |
| RF 2:12-2:13 | Prohibition on discriminatory laws (sex, ethnicity, sexual orientation) | Prevents gendered custody presumptions |
| RF 2:19 | Right to a fair trial | Procedural protections in custody proceedings |

### ECHR Article 8

Sweden ratified the ECHR in 1952. Article 8 protects the right to respect for private and family life, home, and correspondence. Key principles from ECtHR case law applied to custody and contact:

| Principle | Source |
|---|---|
| Mutual enjoyment of parent-child company is a **fundamental element of family life** | ECtHR established principle; *Eriksson v. Sweden* (1989) |
| States must strike a **fair balance** between interests of child and parents | ECtHR general doctrine |
| **Best interests of the child** are of paramount importance in the balancing process | ECtHR general doctrine |
| States have **positive obligations** to facilitate family reunification and parent-child contact | Multiple ECtHR judgments |
| Removal of a child from parental care requires **substantive and procedural justifications** | ECtHR Article 8 case law |

### Barnkonventionen (CRC) -- Incorporated January 1, 2020

The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child was incorporated into Swedish domestic law through **Lag (2018:1197) om FN:s konvention om barnets rattigheter**, effective January 1, 2020.

| CRC Article | Principle | Effect in Swedish Law |
|---|---|---|
| Article 3 | Best interests of the child shall be a primary consideration in all actions | Reinforces FB 6:2a |
| Article 9 | Child shall not be separated from parents against their will except when competent authorities determine separation is necessary for the child's best interest | Reinforces joint custody default |
| Article 12 | Child has right to express views and have them given due weight | Reinforces child's participation in custody proceedings |
| Article 18 | Both parents have common responsibilities for the upbringing of the child; the state shall render appropriate assistance | Reinforces shared parenting infrastructure |
| Article 27 | Every child has right to adequate standard of living; states shall take measures to recover maintenance from parents | Reinforces underhallsstod system |

**Important limitation:** The CRC gained legal status as national law but does not take precedence over national legislation in situations of conflict. It operates as an interpretive guide alongside domestic statutes.

## Forced Heirship / Floor Mechanisms

| Floor | Mechanism | Can It Be Breached? | What Happens If Breached |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Laglott** | 50% of intestate share reserved for each child | Only by forfeiture for murder of testator (AB 15:1) or will tampering (AB 15:2) | Child claims laglott within 6 months; lifetime gifts subject to clawback (AB 7:4) |
| **Joint custody presumption** | Continues after separation as default | Only by judicial finding that joint custody is contrary to child's best interest (FB 6:5) | Non-custodial parent retains contact rights (FB 6:15) |
| **Contact rights (umgangesratt)** | Non-custodial parent retains right to contact; child has right to relationship with both parents | Only if contact would harm the child | Enforcement through vitesforelaggande (penalty fines) and potential custody transfer for sabotage |
| **Underhallsstod (state backstop)** | State pays child support when obligor defaults | No -- Forsakringskassan pays regardless of obligor's conduct | State bears cost; recovers from obligor through Kronofogden |
| **ECHR Art. 8** | Right to respect for family life | Supranational floor: ECtHR judgment is binding | Sweden must amend its law if found in violation |
| **Barnkonventionen (CRC)** | Best interests, non-separation, participation, common responsibility | Domestic law since 2020; interpretive force | Courts and agencies must consider CRC in all decisions affecting children |
| **No imprisonment for support debt** | Abolished 1879; no civil contempt mechanism | N/A | Enforcement is administrative only; the coercive paradox of *Turner v. Rogers* does not arise |

## Anonymized Parallel

In Sweden, the concept of "parental death" via allocation judgment is structurally impossible. The system is designed, at every level, to prevent a parent from being eliminated from a child's life through adversarial legal process.

Joint custody continues after separation as the automatic default (FB 6:5). To deviate from this default, a court must find -- based on the best interest of the child -- that joint custody is contrary to the child's welfare. The burden falls on the party seeking to remove the other parent, not on the parent seeking to remain. Even when sole custody is awarded, the non-custodial parent retains contact rights (umgangesratt) that can only be restricted when necessary for the child's protection. A parent who sabotages the other parent's contact (umgangessabotage) risks losing custody entirely -- the system penalizes exclusion, not contact.

The financial architecture reinforces this. When a parent does not pay child support, Sweden does not imprison them. It abolished debtor's prisons in 1879. Instead, the state (Forsakringskassan) pays the custodial parent directly and pursues recovery from the obligor through administrative means -- wage garnishment and property seizure through Kronofogden. The child is never without support. The coercive paradox of American contempt proceedings -- jailing someone for failure to pay, thereby making payment impossible -- does not exist in the Swedish system.

The inheritance system provides a final floor. A child's laglott (50% of their intestate share) cannot be overridden by will. The only grounds for forfeiture are murder of the testator or tampering with their will. A parent cannot disinherit a child for estrangement, for choosing the other parent, or for any reason other than lethal criminal conduct. Death resets the ledger.

But the most remarkable feature of the Swedish system is not any single legal provision -- it is the infrastructure. 480 days of parental leave with 90 days non-transferable for each parent means both parents build caregiving relationships from birth. Universal daycare means separation does not require one parent to become the sole coordinator of childcare. The state maintenance backstop means a child's financial security does not depend on the goodwill of the non-custodial parent. And alternating residence (vaxelvist boende) is not a contested exception -- it is the lived reality for roughly 35% of Swedish children with separated parents, backed by research from Malin Bergstrom and colleagues at Karolinska Institutet showing that these children report equal or better well-being compared to children in sole-parent arrangements.

The Swedish system is not perfect. The 2006 recalibration was necessary because courts were imposing joint custody in cases involving domestic violence. The 2025 amendment to FB 6:2a gave greater prominence to risk assessment. And the cultural shift that produced 35% alternating residence did not happen overnight -- it took decades of legislative reform, parental leave policy, and changing social norms.

But the architecture is fundamentally different from the American model. In Sweden:

1. The **default** is joint custody, and it survives separation
2. Deviating from the default requires a **judicial finding** based on the child's best interest
3. Even after deviation, **contact rights survive**
4. Contact sabotage is **penalized**, not rewarded
5. Non-payment of support results in **administrative enforcement**, not imprisonment
6. The **state absorbs the payment risk** through Forsakringskassan
7. Children **cannot be disinherited** (laglott)
8. Two **supranational floors** (ECHR Art. 8 and CRC) constrain domestic law
9. **Infrastructure** (parental leave, daycare, mediation) makes shared parenting practically feasible, not merely legally available

In a system where one parent's attorney can draft an allocation judgment that effectively eliminates the other parent from a child's life, Sweden's model serves as the direct counter-example. The Swedish system asks: what would family law look like if the starting premise were that children need both parents, and the burden of proof fell on anyone seeking to change that?
