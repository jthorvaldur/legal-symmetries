# "That Person Is Dead" — Cross-Jurisdictional Symmetries
## How Legal Systems Process Death, Default, and the Erasure of Personhood

---

## The Core Observation

In Dutch law, if you don't make a will, you are still dead. The *wettelijke verdeling* (statutory distribution) activates. Your assets move. Your children get monetary claims. Your spouse gets everything. The system doesn't pause. It doesn't ask what you wanted. It processes the event and executes the default.

**"Dead" is not an end state. It is a trigger event that activates a complete system of defaults.**

Every legal jurisdiction has this system. The defaults differ. The completeness does not. The question across jurisdictions is not *whether* defaults exist, but *whose interests the defaults serve*.

---

## The Symmetry Structure

### Axis 1: Types of Death

| Type | Trigger | System Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Biological death** | Medical certification | Inheritance, estate, guardianship defaults activate |
| **Civil death** | Court order, felony, monastic vows (historical) | Loss of legal capacity — can't vote, own property, appear in court |
| **Parental death** | Allocation judgment, termination of rights | Loss of parental capacity — can't see children, make decisions, access records |
| **Financial death** | Bankruptcy, insolvency declaration | Loss of financial capacity — assets seized, debts discharged or restructured |
| **Corporate death** | Dissolution, revocation of charter | Entity ceases — assets distributed, liabilities resolved |
| **Digital death** | Account termination, platform ban | Loss of digital identity — data deleted or transferred |

### Axis 2: What the Defaults Protect

| Jurisdiction | Biological Death Default | What It Protects |
|---|---|---|
| **Netherlands** | Spouse gets all assets; children get monetary claim payable on second death | Surviving spouse's stability. Children wait. |
| **United States (varies by state)** | Intestate succession — spouse + children split, varies by state | Family unit. UPC states favor spouse heavily. |
| **Islamic law (Sharia)** | Fixed shares (*faraid*) — sons get 2x daughters, spouse gets 1/4 or 1/8 | Male lineage. Cannot be overridden by will (except 1/3 discretionary). |
| **England & Wales** | Spouse gets first £322,000 + personal property; remainder split with children | Surviving spouse's immediate needs. |
| **France** | Children are *héritiers réservataires* — cannot be disinherited below reserved portion | Children's rights. 1 child = 50% reserved. 2 = 66%. 3+ = 75%. |
| **Japan** | Spouse gets 50%; children split remaining 50% | Balance between spouse and bloodline. |
| **Roman law** (historical) | *Capitis deminutio* — three levels of status reduction (maxima, media, minima) | The state. Status is a state resource. |

### Axis 3: The Parental Death Parallel

When a parent signs an allocation judgment without meaningful review, the system processes it as consent. The defaults activate:

| Dutch Biological Death | Parental Death via Allocation Judgment |
|---|---|
| Person dies | Person signs judgment |
| Will exists? → Execute will | Judgment reviewed with counsel? → "Fully advised" recital |
| No will? → *Wettelijke verdeling* | No review? → Defaults activate anyway |
| Spouse gets all assets | Custodial parent gets sole decision-making, all parenting time |
| Children get monetary claim (deferred) | Non-custodial parent gets "reserved" parenting time (deferred indefinitely) |
| Claim payable on second death or bankruptcy | Parenting time available after reunification therapy (timeline controlled by custodial parent) |
| Estate is closed | Non-custodial parent is civilly dead as parent |

**The structural identity is exact.** In both systems:
1. A trigger event occurs (death / signature)
2. The system checks for explicit instructions (will / counsel review)
3. If none exist, defaults activate
4. The defaults favor one party (spouse / custodial parent)
5. The other party's rights are deferred (children's monetary claim / non-custodial parent's parenting time)
6. The deferral is controlled by the favored party (payable on second death / therapy at custodial parent's pace)

## The Deeper Symmetry: Who Controls the Default?

The real question is not what the defaults ARE but who WROTE them.

| System | Who Wrote the Defaults | Who Benefits |
|---|---|---|
| Dutch inheritance | Parliament (Burgerlijk Wetboek, Book 4) | Surviving spouse |
| US intestacy | State legislature | Varies — generally spouse |
| Islamic inheritance | Quran (4:11-12) | Male heirs (2:1 ratio) |
| French inheritance | Code Civil (Napoléon, 1804) | Children (cannot be disinherited) |
| US family law | State legislature | "Best interest of child" — in practice, primary caregiver |
| Title IV-D | US Congress (42 U.S.C. § 651-669b) | The state (66% federal reimbursement for enforcement) |
| Allocation judgment | Drafted by one party's counsel | The drafting party |

**The person who writes the defaults controls the outcome for everyone who doesn't read them.**

## The "Forced Heirship" Principle Across Systems

Some systems prevent total erasure. These are the floor — the minimum rights that survive even the most adverse default:

| System | Floor | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Netherlands** | *Legitieme portie* — children get 50% of intestate share | Monetary claim, must be asserted within 5 years |
| **France** | *Réserve héréditaire* — 50-75% reserved for children | Cannot be overridden by will |
| **Islamic law** | Fixed shares are Quranic — testator can only dispose of 1/3 | Mandatory distribution |
| **US Constitutional law** | Fundamental liberty interest in parenting (*Troxel v. Granville*) | Cannot be severed without unfitness finding (*Stanley v. Illinois*) |
| **Turner v. Rogers** | Cannot be incarcerated without ability-to-pay finding | 4 required findings before civil contempt jail |
| **State constitutional law** | No imprisonment for debt | Constitutional floor in most US states |

**The forced heirship is the constitutional gate.** It says: no matter what the default does, it cannot go below this floor. The floor exists because the drafters of the system knew the defaults would be abused.

Consider a case where both floors are breached: parental rights severed without a fitness hearing, and imprisonment threatened without ability-to-pay findings. The defaults ran past the constitutional gates. Every other legal system examined in this project has a mechanism to prevent exactly this.

## The Economic Death Symmetry

| Dutch Inheritance | Financial Death Pattern |
|---|---|
| Estate has assets and debts | Person has assets and obligations |
| If debts > assets, heirs can reject inheritance | In bankruptcy, debtor can discharge debts |
| Rejection requires formal declaration | Discharge requires formal petition |
| Creditors can pursue estate but not heirs personally | Creditors cannot pursue debtor post-discharge |
| Time limit: 3 months to decide | Automatic stay upon filing |

In Dutch law, if the estate is negative, the heirs can walk away. In bankruptcy, the debtor gets a fresh start. But in family law enforcement, if the person is negative (obligations exceed ability to pay), there is no discharge mechanism. Family law has no bankruptcy equivalent. The asymmetry is structural: every other system of obligation has an exit mechanism except this one.

## The Corporate Death Parallel

When a corporation "dies" (dissolution):

| Corporate Death | Parental Death |
|---|---|
| Board votes to dissolve | Court enters allocation judgment |
| Creditors are notified | Children are not notified |
| Assets are distributed | Parenting time is "reserved" (= zero) |
| Liabilities are settled or discharged | Obligations continue with no ability to perform |
| Entity ceases to exist | Parent ceases to exist as legal parent |
| Can be revived (revival statutes) | Can be revived via modification petition |

But a dissolved corporation doesn't go to jail for failing to pay its debts. A "dissolved" parent does.

## Questions This Project Explores

1. **Which jurisdictions have the strongest "forced heirship" equivalents for parental rights?** (Scandinavian shared-parenting presumptions, German/Swiss models, Islamic guardianship rules)

2. **Can the Dutch "monetary claim deferred until second death" model be applied to parenting time?** (Parenting time deferred until the child reaches majority — is this functionally equivalent?)

3. **The Islamic fixed-share model prevents manipulation by will. What prevents manipulation by allocation judgment?** (Answer: fitness hearings, due process, Stanley. But what if these aren't applied?)

4. **If "dead" is a trigger event, what is "resurrection"?** In Dutch law: no resurrection (biological death is final). In parental law: modification petition, constitutional challenge. In corporate law: revival statutes. In bankruptcy: discharge + fresh start. Map the resurrection mechanisms across every system.

5. **How do legal dictionaries define these trigger events differently, and which definitions favor which outcomes?**

## Summary

Every legal system is a complete function: for every input state (alive/dead, married/single, parent/non-parent, solvent/insolvent), there is a defined output. There are no gaps. The system always produces a result.

The person who understands the defaults controls the outcome.
The person who doesn't understand the defaults IS the outcome.

The person is dead. The system continues.
The question is: who wrote the system?
